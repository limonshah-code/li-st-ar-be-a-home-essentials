## Troubleshoot Your Samsung Dishwasher: Get It Draining Again

Nothing feels more frustrating than opening your Samsung dishwasher after a cycle and finding standing water. This common problem means your dishes are not clean, and you have a messy cleanup. A non-draining dishwasher signals an issue in its drainage system. But do not worry; many of these problems have simple solutions you can handle yourself. I know this situation can feel overwhelming, but I am here to guide you through each step. This article will show you how to identify the cause of the problem and how to get your Samsung dishwasher to drain properly again. We will cover everything from basic checks to more involved component inspections.

### Takeaway

*   **Check Simple Solutions First:** Always begin by ensuring the power is on, the cycle is complete, and the dishwasher is level.
*   **Clean the Filter Regularly:** A clogged filter is the most common reason for drainage issues in Samsung dishwashers.
*   **Inspect Drain Hoses and Air Gaps:** Look for kinks, obstructions, or blockages that prevent water flow.
*   **Address Drain Pump Issues:** Listen for abnormal noises or check for obstructions around the pump impeller.
*   **Verify Sink Drain Connection:** Ensure your sink drain and garbage disposal are clear, as these often connect to your dishwasher.

### Clear Your Samsung Dishwasher Drain

To get your Samsung dishwasher to drain, begin by checking the filter for clogs. Then, inspect the drain hose for kinks or blockages. Next, ensure your sink's garbage disposal is clear if connected. Finally, investigate the drain pump for obstructions or operational issues.

## Initial Checks: Quick Solutions for Standing Water

When you find standing water in your Samsung dishwasher, the first step is always to start with the simplest solutions. These quick checks often resolve the problem without needing complex repairs. I always advise people to confirm these basic elements before diving into more involved troubleshooting. It saves time and effort, and sometimes, the fix is easier than you think. This initial diagnosis helps rule out common, minor issues.

First, ensure your dishwasher has power. Sometimes a tripped circuit breaker or a loose plug can stop a cycle mid-drain. Go to your electrical panel and check the breaker labeled for your dishwasher. If it is tripped, reset it. Also, confirm the dishwasher cord is firmly plugged into the wall outlet. A proper power supply ensures the drain pump receives the necessary electricity to operate.

Next, you should try canceling the current cycle and restarting it. Sometimes, a dishwasher can get stuck in a wash phase. Pressing the "Cancel" or "Drain" button, if available, can force the machine to attempt a drain cycle. If it does not respond, try turning the dishwasher off at the circuit breaker for a few minutes. Then, turn it back on. This resets the control board and can clear minor glitches.

Finally, check if your dishwasher is level. An uneven dishwasher might struggle to drain water properly due to gravity. Water can pool in one section instead of flowing towards the drain. Use a carpenter's level on the top edge of the dishwasher door to verify its position. Adjust the leveling feet at the bottom corners of the unit if needed. Most Samsung dishwashers have adjustable feet that you can twist to raise or lower each corner. Making sure the appliance sits flat can improve drainage efficiency.

If your Samsung dishwasher still has standing water after these checks, you may need to manually remove the water before proceeding. This step ensures safety and gives you better access to internal components. To do this, simply use a towel or a small cup to scoop out as much water as possible. For detailed instructions, you can refer to a guide on [how to drain dishwasher water manually](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-drain-dishwasher-water-manually). Remember to always unplug the dishwasher from the power outlet before doing any manual water removal or internal inspections. Safety is always my top priority.

## Cleaning the Filter: A Common Drain Blocker

A clogged filter is one of the most frequent reasons your Samsung dishwasher will not drain. I often find this to be the primary culprit when I hear from friends about their drainage issues. Food particles, grease, and other debris accumulate in the filter over time. This buildup creates a barrier, stopping water from flowing out of the dishwasher. Cleaning the filter regularly is essential for optimal performance and prevents many drainage problems.

Samsung dishwashers usually have a multi-part filter system located at the bottom of the wash tub. You will typically find it under the lower spray arm. This system usually includes a coarse filter, a fine filter, and sometimes a micro-filter. The coarse filter catches larger food scraps. The fine filter traps smaller particles. Over time, all these parts can get covered in gunk.

To clean the filter, first, remove the lower dish rack to gain easy access. Then, you will usually twist or unclip the filter assembly to remove it. Samsung models often require a quarter-turn counter-clockwise to unlock the cylindrical filter. Once removed, separate the different filter components. Hold them under running water to rinse away all the trapped food debris. You may need a soft brush, like an old toothbrush, to gently scrub away stubborn grime and greasy residue. Avoid using abrasive sponges or harsh chemicals, as these can damage the filter material.

After cleaning, make sure to reassemble the filter correctly. Each part must fit snugly back into its designated spot. Align any arrows or markings if present. Then, twist or clip the entire assembly back into place until it is secure. A loosely fitted filter will not function properly and can cause further issues. I always double-check that it locks firmly. Regular filter cleaning, perhaps once a month or every few weeks depending on usage, prevents most drain clogs. This simple maintenance step can save you a lot of trouble. For more detailed instructions on where exactly your filter is located, you might find this guide helpful: [where is filter in samsung dishwasher](https://homeessentialsguide.com/where-is-filter-in-samsung-dishwasher). This proactive cleaning makes sure your dishwasher always drains smoothly. A clean filter allows water to pass freely, preventing blockages.

## Inspecting the Drain Hose and Air Gap

Once you have checked the filter, the next common area for drainage issues is the drain hose or air gap. These components carry water out of your dishwasher and can become obstructed or incorrectly installed. I have seen many cases where a simple kink in a hose was the only problem. Checking these elements requires a bit of tracing but is usually straightforward. It is important to inspect the entire path water takes after leaving the dishwasher tub.

The drain hose connects your Samsung dishwasher to your kitchen sink's plumbing or garbage disposal. Start by visually inspecting the entire length of this hose. It runs from the back of the dishwasher to under your sink. Look for any visible kinks or sharp bends in the hose. These can restrict water flow, even if there is no physical blockage inside. If you find a kink, gently straighten it out. Ensure the hose has a proper high loop. This "high loop" prevents dirty sink water from draining back into your dishwasher. The high loop means the hose rises above the drain connection point before descending. Secure it with a hose clamp or zip tie if necessary.

Next, you need to check for internal clogs within the drain hose. Disconnect the drain hose from both the dishwasher and the sink/disposal. Place the end of the hose into a bucket. If you have a shop vac, you can try to suction out any debris. Alternatively, you can try flushing water through it. If water does not flow freely, the hose might contain trapped food particles, grease, or even a small foreign object. You can use a long, flexible brush or a plumber's snake to dislodge any blockages. Be careful not to puncture the hose.

Many dishwashers, especially in areas with specific plumbing codes, use an air gap. This small cylindrical device sits on your kitchen countertop near the faucet. It helps prevent sink water from siphoning back into the dishwasher. If your Samsung dishwasher has an air gap, check it for clogs. Remove the cover and inspect for any debris. Often, you will find food particles or mineral deposits blocking the air gap. Clear any obstructions you find using a small tool or a piece of wire. Once cleared, replace the cover. Cleaning the air gap helps ensure proper ventilation and smooth drainage. For more general guidance on clearing these lines, consider resources like [how to clear dishwasher drain line](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-clear-dishwasher-drain-line). This comprehensive check of the drain hose and air gap often reveals the hidden cause of drainage failure.

## Addressing the Drain Pump and Check Valve

If the filter and drain lines are clear, the problem might lie with your Samsung dishwasher's drain pump or check valve. These components are vital for expelling water at the end of a cycle. I know dealing with pumps sounds intimidating, but often, the issue is a simple obstruction, not a faulty part. It is important to understand what these parts do before attempting any inspection. Always disconnect power to the dishwasher before touching any internal components.

The drain pump is an electric motor that pushes water out of the dishwasher. You will usually hear a distinct humming sound when it operates. If you do not hear this sound, or if you hear a grinding noise, the pump might be failing or obstructed. The check valve, on the other hand, is a small one-way flap located near the drain pump. Its job is to prevent dirty water from flowing back into the dishwasher tub after it has been pumped out. Both components are critical for effective drainage.

To inspect the drain pump, you first need to access it. This usually means removing the lower spray arm and filter assembly from inside the tub. Look into the opening where the pump impeller is located. You might see trapped debris like broken glass, small bones, or food particles. These objects can jam the impeller blades, preventing the pump from spinning. Use a pair of needle-nose pliers or tweezers to carefully remove any foreign objects. Be extremely cautious, as there might be sharp items. It is a good idea to wear protective gloves.

Sometimes, the check valve itself can get stuck or clogged. This small flap might be blocked by debris, or it could be worn out. If the check valve is not sealing properly, water can seep back into the tub. While cleaning the pump area, you can often visually inspect the check valve's condition. If it appears damaged or stuck open, it might need replacement. Replacing the check valve is usually a less complex repair than replacing the entire pump.

If the pump is clear of obstructions but still not draining, the pump motor itself might be failing. This usually requires a multimeter to test its electrical resistance (ohms). For guidance on how to test a dishwasher drain pump's resistance, resources like [how many ohms should a dishwasher drain pump have](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-many-ohms-should-a-dishwasher-drain-pump-have) can be very useful. However, if you are not comfortable with electrical testing, it is best to call a qualified appliance technician. A faulty drain pump requires replacement, which can be a more involved repair. Knowing the signs of a bad pump helps you decide on the next steps.

## Checking the Sink Drain and Garbage Disposal

It might seem unrelated, but your kitchen sink's drain and garbage disposal are often directly connected to your Samsung dishwasher's drainage system. A blockage in your sink or disposal can prevent your dishwasher from draining properly. I have seen many instances where clearing the sink solved the dishwasher's problem instantly. It is an often-overlooked connection point that needs your attention. This external check is critical before assuming the problem is internal to the dishwasher.

First, if you have a garbage disposal, ensure it is completely clear and operational. Many Samsung dishwashers drain directly into the garbage disposal unit. If the disposal is full of food scraps or jammed, it cannot accept water from the dishwasher. Run your garbage disposal for about 30 seconds with a strong stream of cold water. This helps clear any existing food waste. Listen for any unusual noises that might indicate a jam. If it hums but does not spin, it might be jammed, and you will need to clear it manually. There is usually a reset button on the bottom of the disposal unit you can press.

A crucial, often forgotten step, especially in new installations, is checking for the knockout plug in the garbage disposal. When a new garbage disposal is installed, it comes with a small plastic plug blocking the dishwasher drain inlet. This plug must be removed before connecting the dishwasher drain hose. If this plug was never removed, your dishwasher's water has nowhere to go. If you recently installed a new disposal and your dishwasher never drained properly, this is a very likely culprit. You will need to disconnect the dishwasher drain hose from the disposal and carefully knock out the plug using a hammer and screwdriver.

Even without a garbage disposal, a severely clogged kitchen sink drain can back up into the dishwasher. If your sink drains slowly or is completely blocked, this can prevent proper dishwasher drainage. Water often takes the path of least resistance. If the main sink drain is blocked, the water from the dishwasher will just sit there. Try pouring a mixture of baking soda and vinegar down your sink drain, followed by hot water, to clear minor clogs. For more severe sink clogs, you might need a drain snake or a chemical drain cleaner. A general guide on [how to clean kitchen drain](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-clean-kitchen-drain) can offer useful tips. Making sure your entire sink plumbing system is free-flowing is a vital step in troubleshooting your dishwasher's drainage issues.

## Understanding Error Codes and Advanced Diagnostics

Modern Samsung dishwashers are smart appliances; they often display error codes when something goes wrong. These codes are not just random letters and numbers. They are diagnostic tools that tell you what part of the dishwasher is having trouble. When your Samsung dishwasher will not drain, checking for error codes on the display panel can save you a lot of guesswork. I always tell people to consult the user manual for a full list, but some codes commonly relate to drainage issues.

Common Samsung error codes related to drainage include "5C," "5E," "OC," or "OE." The specific code can vary slightly between models, so always verify it with your appliance's manual.
*   **5C/5E:** These codes often indicate a drainage error. They suggest that water is not draining correctly or quickly enough. This could point to a clogged filter, a kinked drain hose, or a problem with the drain pump.
*   **OC/OE:** These codes usually mean an overflow or an excessive amount of water detected in the tub. This happens if water cannot drain out, causing it to back up. It is similar to the 5C/5E codes, indicating a drainage obstruction.

When you see an error code, the first thing to do is try a simple reset. Turn off the dishwasher at the power button, wait a minute, and then turn it back on. If the code persists, you should then proceed with the physical checks we discussed earlier: the filter, drain hose, air gap, and sink drain. The error code simply confirms there is a drain issue; it does not magically fix the blockage. Think of it as a signal, not a solution.

For more advanced diagnostics, some Samsung dishwashers have a test mode or service mode. Entering this mode can sometimes help identify which specific component is failing. The steps to enter service mode vary by model and are usually found in the service manual, not the user manual. This typically involves a specific sequence of button presses. In service mode, the dishwasher cycles through its components, and you can observe their function. For example, it might activate the drain pump, allowing you to listen if it turns on or if it sounds unusual.

However, I must stress that going into service mode and interpreting its results can be complex. If you are not comfortable with advanced diagnostics, it is better to stick to the basic troubleshooting steps. Incorrectly using service mode or misinterpreting codes can sometimes lead to more issues. If an error code consistently appears after you have checked all the common culprits, it is a strong indicator that a specific component, like the drain pump or a sensor, might be faulty. At this point, it is often best to prepare for a part replacement or consider calling a professional technician.

## When to Call a Professional: Knowing Your Limits

While many Samsung dishwasher draining issues are fixable with DIY methods, there comes a point where calling a professional is the smartest and safest option. I always encourage people to try basic troubleshooting first. But knowing your limits prevents further damage to your appliance or, more importantly, injury to yourself. It is crucial to recognize when a problem is beyond your comfort or skill level. Attempting complex repairs without proper knowledge or tools can be risky.

You should consider calling a professional appliance technician if:

*   **You have tried all the troubleshooting steps without success.** If you have meticulously checked the filter, drain hose, air gap, sink drain, garbage disposal, and even inspected the drain pump for obstructions, and your Samsung dishwasher still refuses to drain, the issue is likely more complex. This suggests a deeper problem, such as a faulty drain pump motor, a broken check valve, or a malfunctioning control board. These repairs often require specialized tools and diagnostic equipment.
*   **You are uncomfortable with electrical work.** Working with electrical components, like testing a drain pump motor with a multimeter, can be dangerous if you do not know what you are doing. There is a risk of electric shock. If you feel hesitant about any step involving electrical components, please stop and call an expert. Safety should always be your number one priority.
*   **Your dishwasher is displaying persistent, unresolvable error codes.** While error codes point to issues, if you cannot clear them after basic troubleshooting and resets, it indicates a component failure. A technician can accurately diagnose the specific part that needs replacement. They also have access to genuine Samsung parts and the expertise to install them correctly.
*   **You suspect a major component failure.** If you hear no sound from the drain pump, or if it is making grinding noises but not draining, the pump might be dead. Replacing a drain pump or a control board is a significant repair that often warrants professional assistance. These parts can be expensive, and you want to ensure they are installed correctly to avoid repeat issues.
*   **The repair cost outweighs the appliance's value.** A technician can provide an estimate for the repair. Sometimes, especially with older Samsung dishwashers, the cost of a major repair might be close to the cost of a new, more efficient model. A professional can help you decide if repair or replacement is the better option.

A professional technician has the experience to quickly diagnose complex issues. They carry common replacement parts and tools. They also ensure the repair is done safely and correctly, often offering a warranty on their work. Do not hesitate to seek expert help when the situation calls for it.

## FAQ Section

### Why is my Samsung dishwasher not draining completely?

A Samsung dishwasher often does not drain completely due to a partial clog. The most common cause is a dirty or obstructed filter at the bottom of the tub. Food particles, grease, and debris accumulate there, slowing down or stopping water flow. Another reason could be a partially kinked drain hose, which restricts water from fully exiting the machine.

### How often should I clean my Samsung dishwasher filter?

You should clean your Samsung dishwasher filter regularly, ideally once a month. If you frequently wash heavily soiled dishes or have a large household, cleaning it every two weeks might be better. This simple maintenance task prevents food particles from building up and causing drainage problems. Regular cleaning ensures optimal performance.

### Can a clogged air gap stop a Samsung dishwasher from draining?

Yes, a clogged air gap can definitely stop a Samsung dishwasher from draining. The air gap is a small device on your kitchen counter that prevents dirty sink water from flowing back into the dishwasher. If food debris or mineral deposits block this component, it creates a vacuum seal, preventing the dishwasher from expelling water. Clearing the air gap restores proper drainage.

### What do error codes "5C" or "OC" mean on a Samsung dishwasher?

Error codes "5C" (or "5E") and "OC" (or "OE") on a Samsung dishwasher both indicate a drainage issue. "5C/5E" specifically means the dishwasher is failing to drain. "OC/OE" suggests an overflow condition, where water is detected as being too high because it cannot drain out. Both codes point to a blockage or a problem with the drain system.

### Is it safe to manually drain my Samsung dishwasher?

Yes, it is safe to manually drain your Samsung dishwasher, but you must take precautions. Always disconnect the dishwasher from its power source before attempting to remove any standing water. Use a towel or a cup to scoop out the water carefully. This step helps prevent electric shock and makes it safer to inspect internal components.

### How long does a Samsung dishwasher drain pump last?

A Samsung dishwasher drain pump typically lasts between 7 to 10 years, depending on usage and maintenance. However, its lifespan can shorten if the pump is frequently overworked due to clogs in the filter or drain line. Signs of a failing pump include unusual noises during the drain cycle, or the pump not activating at all.
