## Can You Wash Hockey Gear in a Washing Machine?

Every hockey player knows the distinct smell that follows them off the ice. That strong odor comes from sweat and bacteria building up on your equipment. You probably ask yourself: "Can I wash hockey equipment in the washing machine to make it fresh again?" It is a common question.

This article gives you clear answers. We will explore which parts of your hockey gear are safe for machine washing. We will also tell you how to prepare your equipment. You will learn the correct washing machine settings to use. We cover the entire process from start to finish. We also highlight which items should never go near a washing machine. This guide helps you keep your gear clean and extend its life.

### Takeaway

*   Most soft hockey equipment is safe for machine washing.
*   Always remove hard inserts and plastic pieces before washing.
*   Use a gentle cycle with cold water and a mild detergent.
*   Never machine wash helmets, skates, or specialized gloves.
*   Air dry all hockey equipment thoroughly after washing.

Yes, you can wash many pieces of hockey equipment in the washing machine. Items like shoulder pads, elbow pads, shin guards (after removing hard shells), jocks, jerseys, and socks often do well in a gentle wash cycle. Always check the care labels first. Proper machine settings and air drying are key for successful cleaning.

### Understanding Your Gear: What Can Go In?

Many parts of your hockey equipment accumulate sweat and bacteria. This causes bad smells. It is important to know which items are safe to wash in a washing machine. Putting the wrong gear in can cause damage. We want to avoid costly replacements.

#### Soft Pads and Jerseys

Most soft, fabric-based hockey equipment is machine-washable. This includes your jersey, socks, and base layers. Shoulder pads, elbow pads, and shin pad liners often fit this category too. These items are designed to absorb sweat. They benefit greatly from a deep clean. Always check for removable hard plastic or foam inserts first. You need to take these out before washing.

#### Hard Shell Items and Skates

Some hockey gear has hard plastic shells or rigid components. These items typically do not belong in a washing machine. Helmets, skates, and goalie pads are prime examples. Their structure can break down in water or agitation. The washing machine can also damage the machine itself. For a detailed guide on cleaning all your gear, including those not suited for the machine, visit our article on [how to clean hockey equipment](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-clean-hockey-equipment).

Here is a list of common hockey equipment that is generally safe for machine washing:

*   **Jerseys:** Game jerseys, practice jerseys.
*   **Socks:** Hockey socks, athletic socks.
*   **Base Layers:** Undergarments, compression shorts, shirts.
*   **Jocks/Jills:** Protective shorts/briefs.
*   **Shoulder Pads:** Ensure all hard plastic inserts are removed.
*   **Elbow Pads:** Take out any rigid components.
*   **Shin Guard Liners:** Remove the hard plastic shells first.
*   **Girdles:** If they do not have hard, non-removable inserts.
*   **Hockey Pants/Breezers:** Only if they are the fabric shell type without hard padding.

Always remember to use a mesh laundry bag for smaller items. This protects them and prevents snagging. It also keeps them from getting lost. Follow these simple rules to keep your gear clean and in good shape.

### Preparing Hockey Equipment for the Wash

Proper preparation is vital before you wash hockey equipment in the washing machine. Skipping these steps can damage your gear. It can also harm your washing machine. Taking a few extra minutes now saves trouble later.

#### Removing Loose Parts

Before washing, check every piece of equipment carefully. Many pads have removable plastic inserts or foam pieces. These parts protect you during play. However, they can warp or break in the washing machine. For instance, shin guards often have a hard outer shell that detaches. You must take this off. Similarly, some shoulder pads have plastic caps that unclip. Ensure all buckles, straps, and Velcro fasteners are secured. This prevents them from snagging other items or the machine's agitator.

#### Spot Treating Stains

Hockey involves a lot of action, which often leads to stains. Blood, dirt, and puck marks are common. You should spot treat these areas before the main wash. Use a mild stain remover or a mix of gentle detergent and water. Apply it directly to the stain. Let it sit for about 15-30 minutes. Gently scrub the area with a soft brush or cloth. This pre-treatment helps lift tough stains. It ensures your gear comes out cleaner. Always test any stain remover on a small, hidden area first. This checks for colorfastness.

Here is a simple checklist for preparing your gear:

*   **Disassemble:** Remove all hard plastic, foam inserts, or non-washable parts.
*   **Secure:** Close all Velcro straps, zippers, and buckles.
*   **Shake Out:** Get rid of loose dirt, tape bits, or debris.
*   **Inspect:** Check for tears or loose stitching that might worsen in the wash.
*   **Spot Treat:** Apply stain remover to any visible marks.
*   **Bag It:** Place smaller items like elbow pads or jocks into a mesh laundry bag. This protects delicate items. It also stops straps from tangling.

By following these steps, you ensure your equipment is ready for a safe and effective wash. This helps maintain its condition and extend its lifespan. It also protects your appliance from harm.

### Choosing the Right Washing Machine Settings

Using the correct settings on your washing machine is crucial for hockey equipment. The wrong settings can damage your gear. They can also reduce its protective qualities. I always make sure to select settings that are gentle yet effective.

#### Cycle Selection

For most hockey equipment, a gentle or delicate cycle is best. This cycle uses less agitation. It prevents stretching, tearing, or damage to padding. Some machines also have a "bulky items" or "heavy duty" cycle. Avoid these if they use high agitation. A "sports wear" cycle, if available, is also a good choice. These cycles are designed for synthetic fabrics often found in athletic gear. Remember, we want to clean, not destroy.

#### Water Temperature Decisions

Cold water is almost always the best option for washing hockey equipment. Hot water can cause several problems. It can shrink some synthetic fabrics. It can also break down the foam and padding materials. Hot water might also set in certain stains, making them harder to remove. Cold water cleans effectively without these risks. It helps preserve the integrity of your gear. If you are unsure about water temperatures, you can learn more about specific settings like [what is hot wash in washing machine](https://homeessentialsguide.com/what-is-hot-wash-in-washing-machine) for different garment types. Using cold water also saves energy, which is a bonus.

#### Detergent Choices

Choose a mild, gentle laundry detergent. Avoid harsh chemicals or bleach. Bleach can weaken fabric fibers and degrade padding. It can also cause discoloration. An athletic detergent designed to remove odors is a good option. These detergents often target sweat and bacteria more effectively. For especially smelly gear, you can add a cup of white vinegar to the wash cycle. Vinegar is a natural deodorizer. It helps break down odor-causing bacteria. Use less detergent than you would for a full load of clothes. Too much detergent can leave residue on your gear.

Here is a summary of recommended settings:

*   **Cycle:** Delicate, Gentle, or Sports Wear.
*   **Water Temperature:** Cold water only.
*   **Detergent:** Mild, gentle, or athletic-specific detergent. Add white vinegar for odor control if needed.
*   **Load Size:** Do not overload your machine. Hockey equipment is bulky. Ensure there is enough space for items to move freely. This allows proper cleaning. If you are unsure about load sizes, a general guide like [how full washing machine should be](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-full-washing-machine-should-be) can help you understand the right capacity. By selecting these settings, you give your hockey equipment the best chance for a thorough and safe wash. You also protect your appliance.

### The Washing Process: Step-by-Step Guide

Once you have prepared your hockey equipment and chosen the right settings, you are ready to wash. Following these steps helps ensure a smooth and effective cleaning process. I always find it helpful to have a clear plan.

#### Loading the Machine Safely

Before loading, remember the general rule: do not overload the washing machine. Hockey gear is bulky. It needs space to move freely during the wash cycle. This ensures proper cleaning. Overloading can lead to incomplete cleaning. It can also strain your machine. Place larger items like shoulder pads directly into the drum. Put smaller items like elbow pads or jocks into a mesh laundry bag first. This protects them. It also prevents straps from tangling. If your machine has an agitator, arrange items evenly around it. For impeller machines, spread items loosely. This balances the load. A balanced load prevents the machine from shaking excessively.

#### Running the Cycle

With the machine loaded and settings selected, you can start the wash cycle. Add your chosen mild detergent. If you are using white vinegar for extra odor removal, add it during the rinse cycle or directly into the detergent dispenser before starting. Run the cold water, delicate cycle as planned. Stay nearby for the first few minutes to ensure everything runs smoothly. Listen for unusual noises or excessive shaking. If the machine seems off balance, pause it. Redistribute the items. Then restart the cycle. Once the cycle finishes, remove the equipment promptly. Leaving wet gear in the machine can lead to mildew growth. This undoes all your hard work.

Here is a simple flow for the washing process:

1.  **Load Carefully:** Place large items directly, small items in mesh bags.
2.  **Balance Load:** Distribute items evenly in the drum.
3.  **Add Detergent:** Use a mild, low-sudsing detergent.
4.  **Add Vinegar (Optional):** Pour white vinegar into the dispenser or during the rinse.
5.  **Start Cycle:** Run the delicate/gentle cycle with cold water.
6.  **Monitor:** Check for balance during the initial spin.
7.  **Remove Promptly:** Take out equipment as soon as the cycle ends.

Following these steps ensures your hockey equipment gets a good wash. It comes out clean and ready for proper drying. This method helps maintain the quality and longevity of your protective gear.

### Drying Your Hockey Equipment Properly

After washing your hockey equipment, the drying phase is just as important. Incorrect drying can damage gear. It can also lead to mildew and lingering smells. Air drying is the only safe method for most hockey items.

#### The Importance of Air Drying

You should never put hockey equipment in a machine dryer. The high heat from a dryer can cause several problems. It can melt foam padding. It can shrink fabrics. It can also damage the adhesive that holds many pads together. The tumbling action can also distort the shape of protective gear. This reduces its effectiveness. Air drying allows your equipment to dry naturally and safely. It preserves the shape and materials. This helps your gear last longer. It also prevents the growth of mold and mildew.

#### Setting Up a Drying Area

Find a well-ventilated area for drying. A basement with a fan, a garage, or even outdoors on a sunny day works well. Hang each piece of equipment separately. Use strong hangers or a drying rack. Ensure air can circulate around every item. This speeds up the drying process. Spread out pads and open up any adjustable straps. This allows air to reach all surfaces. Placing a fan nearby can also help. It moves air and reduces drying time. It also helps prevent odors from settling in. Depending on humidity levels, drying can take 24-48 hours. Make sure everything is completely dry before storing it. Even slightly damp gear can develop mildew.

Here are key points for drying:

*   **No Machine Dryer:** Avoid all machine drying for hockey equipment.
*   **Ventilated Space:** Choose an area with good air circulation.
*   **Hang Separately:** Use hangers or racks to allow air flow around each item.
*   **Open Pads:** Extend straps and open up padded areas.
*   **Use a Fan:** A fan speeds up drying and improves air circulation.
*   **Complete Dry:** Ensure every piece is bone dry before putting it away.

By air drying your hockey equipment correctly, you keep it fresh and extend its useful life. This care maintains the gear's protective qualities too. A properly dried kit is a fresh kit.

### Items You Should Never Wash in the Machine

While a washing machine offers convenience, some hockey equipment must never go near it. These items require different cleaning methods. Putting them in the wash can cause irreparable damage. It can also compromise your safety during play.

#### Helmets and Face Cages

Your helmet is your most important piece of protective gear. It is designed to absorb impact. Machine washing a helmet can weaken its internal foam. It can loosen adhesives. It can also damage the shell. These damages are often not visible. Yet, they reduce the helmet's ability to protect your head. Face cages and visors can bend or scratch in a washing machine. Always clean your helmet by hand. Use a mild soap and water solution. Wipe down the shell and internal padding. Air dry thoroughly.

#### Skates and Blades

Hockey skates are a complex piece of equipment. They have stiff boots, delicate blades, and often heat-moldable liners. Washing machines can warp the boot. They can damage the blade holders. They can also cause the steel blades to rust. The felt tongue can also become waterlogged and stiff. Skates need careful hand cleaning. Wipe the blades dry after every use to prevent rust. Clean the boots with a damp cloth and mild soap. Air dry them completely away from direct heat.

#### Specialized Gloves and Sticks

Hockey gloves are another item to keep out of the washing machine. Many gloves have leather palms or specialized synthetic materials. These can stiffen, crack, or degrade in the wash. The padding can also clump. This reduces comfort and protection. Sticks are, of course, never washed. They are made of composite materials or wood. Water exposure is not good for them. For gloves, hand wash them gently with mild soap and water. You can also use a specialized glove cleaner. Let them air dry. You can stuff them with newspaper to help them keep their shape. Here is a quick list of items to always keep out of the washing machine:

*   **Helmets and Cages:** Crucial for head protection, easily damaged by water and agitation.
*   **Skates:** Boots warp, blades rust, liners degrade.
*   **Hockey Gloves:** Leather and specialized materials can stiffen or crack.
*   **Sticks:** Not designed for water exposure.
*   **Goaltender Pads:** Too bulky and complex to machine wash.
*   **Blades/Hard Shells:** Any hard plastic or metal component that you removed from soft pads.

Treating these items with care ensures they last longer. It also keeps you safe on the ice. Hand washing and proper drying are key for these specific pieces.

### Maintenance Tips for Fresh Hockey Gear

Keeping your hockey equipment clean does not stop after a wash. Regular maintenance between washes is essential. This helps control odors and extends the life of your gear. I always try to make these steps a part of my post-game routine.

#### Post-Game Routine

The most important step to keep your gear fresh is to air it out immediately after every practice or game. Do not leave sweaty equipment stuffed in your hockey bag. This creates a perfect breeding ground for bacteria and mold. As soon as you get home, take everything out of the bag. Hang each piece separately. A dedicated drying rack or even a shower curtain rod works well. This allows air to circulate around all sides of the equipment. If possible, wipe down hard surfaces like helmets and skates with a damp cloth. A quick spray with an odor-neutralizing spray can also help between deep washes. This simple routine makes a big difference in preventing smells.

#### Smart Storage Solutions

Proper storage is also key for maintaining fresh hockey gear. Once your equipment is completely dry, store it in a cool, dry place. Avoid leaving it in a hot car trunk or a damp basement. These environments encourage bacterial growth. A breathable hockey bag is better than a sealed plastic bin. It allows some air circulation. Some players use activated charcoal inserts or dryer sheets in their bags. These help absorb lingering odors. Make sure the storage area is free from direct sunlight. UV rays can degrade materials over time. By combining good post-game habits with smart storage, you can keep your hockey equipment smelling better for longer.

Here are the simple tips for fresh gear:

*   **Always Air Out:** Remove all gear from your bag immediately after playing.
*   **Hang Separately:** Allow maximum airflow around each item.
*   **Wipe Down:** Clean hard surfaces like helmets and skates.
*   **Odor Spray:** Use an odor neutralizer between washes.
*   **Dry Storage:** Store fully dry gear in a cool, dry, breathable space.
*   **Avoid Heat/Damp:** Do not store gear in hot cars or wet areas.
*   **Odor Absorbers:** Use charcoal or dryer sheets in your bag.

Implementing these maintenance tips helps reduce the need for frequent deep washes. It also ensures your equipment stays in top condition. This means a more pleasant experience for you and everyone around you.

### Frequently Asked Questions

People often have many questions about cleaning hockey equipment. Here are answers to some common concerns.

#### How often should I wash my hockey equipment?

The frequency depends on how often you play and how much you sweat. For players who play several times a week, a full machine wash every 2-4 weeks is good. Airing out gear after every use is more important. This regular airing reduces the need for constant deep cleaning.

#### Can I use regular laundry detergent?

Yes, a mild, regular laundry detergent is usually fine. Avoid heavy-duty detergents with strong perfumes or harsh chemicals. These can irritate skin or damage delicate materials. Athletic-specific detergents are also a good choice. They often target sweat odors effectively.

#### What about fabric softener?

You should avoid using fabric softener on hockey equipment. Fabric softeners can leave a residue on the fibers. This can clog the material's pores. It can reduce its breathability. It can also trap bacteria and make future washes less effective. Just stick to detergent.

#### Can I wash hockey gloves in the washing machine?

No, you should not wash hockey gloves in a washing machine. Machine washing can damage the leather palms. It can also ruin the internal padding. It can make them stiff and uncomfortable. Hand wash gloves with a mild soap and then air dry them.

#### What if my equipment still smells after washing?

If your equipment still smells after washing, the odor might be deeply set. You can try adding a cup of white vinegar to the rinse cycle during the next wash. You can also try a specialized sports odor eliminator. Ensure equipment is completely dry before storing it.

#### Is it safe to wash knee pads in a washing machine?

Many knee pads have soft fabric sleeves with removable hard plastic caps. If the hard plastic cap can be removed, the fabric sleeve is usually safe to machine wash. Use a gentle cycle and cold water. Always remove the hard plastic first. Air dry the fabric part completely.

### Conclusion

Tackling the challenge of smelly hockey equipment is simpler than you might think. We have found that you can wash hockey equipment in the washing machine. This applies to many fabric-based items. These items include jerseys, socks, and most soft pads. You must remove hard inserts first. This step protects both your gear and your washing machine.

Remember to choose a gentle cycle with cold water. Use a mild detergent. Always air dry your equipment completely. Never use a machine dryer. Items like helmets, skates, and specialty gloves should stay out of the washing machine. They need hand washing. By following these clear steps, you will keep your gear clean and fresh. This extends its life. It also makes your time on the ice more enjoyable. Make these cleaning and maintenance habits a part of your routine. You will notice a big difference. Say goodbye to that infamous hockey smell!
