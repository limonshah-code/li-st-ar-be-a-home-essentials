### What does sanitize do on a dishwasher?

The sanitize cycle on a dishwasher uses very hot water, typically reaching temperatures of 150-155°F (65-68°C) or higher. This high heat effectively kills up to 99.9% of bacteria and other microorganisms on dishes. It provides a deeper level of hygiene than a standard wash cycle, meeting strict health and safety standards.

#### How Heat Affects Microorganisms

Heat is a very effective germ killer. When bacteria and viruses are exposed to high temperatures, their essential components break down. Proteins, which are vital for their survival and function, denature. This means they lose their normal shape and can no longer work. Enzymes, which are types of proteins, stop functioning. The cell walls of bacteria can also be damaged or rupture. This leads to the death of the microorganism.

The effectiveness of heat depends on two main factors: temperature and exposure time. A very high temperature for a short time can be effective. A slightly lower temperature for a longer time can also achieve the same result. Dishwasher sanitize cycles are carefully calibrated to find the right balance. They maximize germ killing while ensuring safety for your dishes. This scientific approach guarantees a high level of hygiene. It provides peace of mind with every wash.

### Common Misconceptions About Dishwasher Sanitization

Many people have ideas about how dishwashers work. Some of these ideas are not always correct. It is important to clear up common misconceptions about dishwasher sanitization. This helps you use your appliance effectively. It also protects your family's health. Let me tackle some of these misunderstandings directly.

One common belief is that all dishwasher cycles sanitize dishes. This is not true. A regular wash cycle cleans dishes. It removes food and grease. However, it does not always reach the high temperatures required for sanitization. Only cycles specifically labeled "Sanitize" or "SaniRinse" achieve this. These cycles have a dedicated high-temperature phase. They hold the heat for a set time to kill germs. Your normal wash may be hot, but it often falls short of true sanitization standards.

Another misconception is that dish soap itself sanitizes. Dish soap is designed to clean, not sanitize. It breaks down grease and food. It helps lift particles from surfaces. Dish soap does not contain germ-killing agents strong enough for sanitization. The hot water in the sanitize cycle is what kills the germs. Using extra soap will not make your dishes more sanitary. It can actually leave a residue. You might be surprised to learn that [does dish soap sanitize](https://homeessentialsguide.com/does-dish-soap-sanitize)? The answer is no, not in the way a sanitize cycle does. Soap primarily cleans, while heat sanitizes.

Some people also believe that "sanitize" means "sterile." This is not entirely accurate. Sanitization significantly reduces the number of harmful bacteria and microorganisms. It lowers them to a safe level. This is typically a 99.9% reduction. Sterilization, on the other hand, aims to eliminate all forms of microbial life. This is a much higher standard, usually achieved through specialized equipment or extreme conditions. While the sanitize cycle is highly effective, it does not make your dishes sterile. It makes them hygienically safe for everyday use.

Finally, some think that if their dishwasher has a sanitize option, it will always be active. This is not true. You must select the sanitize option manually. It is an additional setting. It is not part of every default wash cycle. Always check your dishwasher's control panel. Make sure you select the sanitize option when you need it. Understanding these points helps you make informed choices about your dishwashing routines. It ensures you get the most out of your appliance's features.

### FAQ Section

#### Q1: Is the sanitize cycle necessary every time I wash dishes?
No, the sanitize cycle is not necessary for every load of dishes. It is a powerful feature for specific situations. Use it when you need extra germ protection. This includes after handling raw meat, when family members are sick, or for baby items. For everyday loads, a regular wash cycle is usually sufficient.

#### Q2: Does the sanitize cycle use more energy or water?
Yes, the sanitize cycle typically uses more energy than a standard wash. This is because it heats the water to a much higher temperature. It also maintains that temperature for a longer period. It may also extend the cycle duration. While it uses more energy, the benefits of improved hygiene can outweigh the extra cost.

#### Q3: Can I sanitize all my dishware using this cycle?
Most heat-safe dishware is suitable for the sanitize cycle. This includes ceramic, glass, and most stainless steel items. However, some delicate plastics or antique dishes might not withstand the extremely high temperatures. Always check the manufacturer's recommendations for your dishware. Look for "dishwasher safe" and "heat safe" labels.

#### Q4: How do I know if my dishwasher successfully sanitized my dishes?
Many dishwashers with a sanitize option have an indicator light. This light often says "Sanitized" or "Cycle Complete." It illuminates when the cycle has met the required temperature and duration. If your dishwasher does not have this light, check your user manual. It will provide details on how to confirm sanitization.

#### Q5: What if my dishwasher does not have a sanitize option?
If your dishwasher lacks a sanitize option, you can still achieve a good level of hygiene. You can manually sanitize dishes. Boil water in a large pot. Carefully submerge clean dishes for several minutes. Ensure they are heat-safe. Alternatively, use a sanitizing solution if suitable for your dishware.

#### Q6: Can the sanitize cycle damage delicate dishes or plastics?
Yes, the sanitize cycle's high temperatures can potentially damage certain delicate items. Plastics not labeled "dishwasher safe" or "heat resistant" may warp or melt. Fine china, crystal, or hand-painted items could also be damaged. Always prioritize the dishware manufacturer's care instructions to avoid damage.
