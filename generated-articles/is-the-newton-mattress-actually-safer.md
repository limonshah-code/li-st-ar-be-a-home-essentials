### Understanding Infant Sleep Safety: Why Mattresses Matter

Ensuring a safe sleep environment for infants is a top priority for all parents. Safe sleep practices help reduce the risk of Sudden Infant Death Syndrome, or SIDS. The mattress a baby sleeps on plays a crucial role in this safety equation. A mattress should be firm, fit the crib snugly, and allow proper airflow.

Traditional crib mattresses vary widely in materials and breathability. Some mattresses use foam, while others have springs. These materials often trap heat and restrict air circulation. A baby's face pressing against such a surface can lead to a dangerous situation. Limited airflow increases the risk of rebreathing exhaled air. This rebreathing can reduce oxygen levels and increase carbon dioxide, posing a threat to infants.

This is where the design of the mattress becomes important. Experts recommend a firm sleep surface for babies. Soft surfaces can pose suffocation risks and are not recommended. Choosing the right mattress means looking beyond just comfort for the baby. It means evaluating how the mattress supports safe breathing and overall well-being. For more information on choosing safe options, consider what mattress for baby is best suited for your infant.

Parents carefully research crib mattresses to find the safest option. They want a mattress that offers both support and peace of mind. The mattress must also be free from harmful chemicals. Our focus shifts from simple comfort to vital safety features. The core function of a baby's mattress is to provide a secure and healthy sleep space.

### Comparing Newton: How It Stacks Against Traditional Crib Mattresses

When considering a crib mattress, parents often weigh options against traditional models. Traditional crib mattresses usually come in two main types: foam or innerspring. Foam mattresses use a solid block of foam, often polyurethane. Innerspring mattresses have coiled springs covered by layers of padding and fabric. Both types have been widely used for decades.

The main difference lies in breathability and material composition. Traditional foam mattresses, while firm, can be dense. They trap heat and do not allow air to pass through easily. If a baby rolls onto their stomach, their face might be pressed against a non-breathable surface. This significantly increases the risk of rebreathing exhaled air. Innerspring mattresses, despite having some internal air pockets, also rely on top layers of padding. These layers can still impede airflow directly where the baby breathes.

Newton’s Wovenaire core offers a distinct advantage here. Its 90% air volume means air flows freely through the entire mattress. You can actually breathe right through the material yourself. This difference is tangible and directly addresses the concern of rebreathing carbon dioxide. Studies show that Newton mattresses allow significantly more airflow compared to conventional foam or innerspring models. This can be a critical safety feature for infants.

Another comparison point is washability. Traditional mattresses are difficult, if not impossible, to thoroughly clean beyond surface wiping. Spills, accidents, and bacteria can accumulate deep within the mattress layers. The Newton mattress, on the other hand, allows you to wash the entire core and cover. This complete washability ensures a truly hygienic sleep surface for your baby. This significantly reduces exposure to allergens, mold, and bacteria. The ability to deeply clean the mattress adds another layer of health and safety.
