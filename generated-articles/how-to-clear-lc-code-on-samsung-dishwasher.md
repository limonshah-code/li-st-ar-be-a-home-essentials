This code indicates a detected leak, and your machine stops operation. I understand how concerning this can be. You want your dishes clean, and you need your appliance to work correctly.

This guide will show you exactly how to clear the LC code on your Samsung dishwasher. We will look at what the LC code means. We will cover common causes of leaks. *   Power cycle the dishwasher first to reset the system.

## Understanding the Samsung Dishwasher LC Code

You saw the "LC" code light up on your Samsung dishwasher display. This code is not random. It stands for "Leakage Check" or "Leak Detected." Your dishwasher has built-in sensors designed to find water where it should not be. These sensors live in a pan at the bottom of the appliance.

When water hits this pan, the sensor trips. The dishwasher stops its cycle. It then displays the LC code. This is a safety feature. It prevents water damage to your kitchen floor. The machine protects your home from a potential flood. Recognizing this code is the first step toward a solution.

The LC code indicates a problem requiring attention. It does not mean your dishwasher is broken beyond repair. Often, the cause is simple. You can fix many leak issues yourself. Understanding this code helps you approach the problem calmly. This information also guides your troubleshooting efforts. For more details on what this specific error means, you can read [what does LC mean on a Samsung dishwasher](https://homeessentialsguide.com/what-does-lc-mean-on-a-samsung-dishwasher). Remember, the flashing or constant LC code needs your quick action. If your Samsung dishwasher is [blinking LC](https://homeessentialsguide.com/why-is-my-samsung-dishwasher-blinking-lc), it means the leak sensor is actively triggered, demanding immediate investigation.

## Initial Steps to Clear LC Code: Power Reset

When an error code appears, my first step is always a power reset. This simple action can resolve many temporary glitches. Dishwashers, like computers, sometimes have minor software hiccups. A quick reset can clear these errors. It tells the machine to restart its internal systems.

To perform a power reset, you must cut the power completely. Go to your home's circuit breaker box. Find the breaker labeled for your kitchen or dishwasher. Turn this breaker off. Leave the power off for at least 5 to 10 minutes. This allows the internal components to fully discharge.

After waiting, turn the breaker back on. Return to your dishwasher. Check if the LC code is still present. If the code cleared, it was likely a temporary sensor error. You can then try running a short wash cycle. This confirms the problem is gone. Many users find this step effectively [resets their Samsung dishwasher LC code](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-reset-samsung-dishwasher-lc-code). If the code comes back, a real leak exists. You need to investigate further.

## Investigating Common Causes of the LC Code

A persistent LC code means a physical leak is present. We need to find the source. This step requires careful observation. Small leaks can be hard to spot. You should always begin by turning off power to the dishwasher before any physical inspection. This ensures your safety while you work on the appliance.

You can often fix common issues that cause the LC code. Do not worry if you do not immediately see water. Sometimes, it is a very small drip. The sensor is sensitive. It triggers even with minimal moisture. Many people wonder [why their Samsung dishwasher has an LC code but no leak](https://homeessentialsguide.com/why-does-my-samsung-dishwasher-have-an-lc-code-but-no-leak). This usually means the leak is hidden, or very small, perhaps only during a specific cycle. You will need to look closely to identify the problem. For more general guidance on resolving the issue, see [how to fix a Samsung dishwasher with an LC code](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-fix-samsung-dishwasher-with-lc-code).

### Check the Dishwasher Door and Gasket

First, look at the dishwasher door. A faulty door seal is a common leak point. The rubber gasket around the door opening should be clean and flexible. Examine it for cracks, tears, or food debris. Old gaskets can become brittle. Food particles can prevent a tight seal.

Clean the gasket with a damp cloth. Remove any gunk or buildup. Run your finger along the entire seal. Feel for damaged spots. If the gasket looks worn, you may need to replace it. A loose or damaged gasket allows water to escape. This water then drips into the overflow pan.

### Examine Hoses and Connections

Next, inspect the hoses. Your dishwasher has water inlet hoses and a drain hose. These connect to the machine and to your plumbing. Pull the dishwasher out slightly if you can. Look behind and under the unit. Check all hose connections for drips or wet spots.

The supply lines bring water in. The drain hose carries water out. Ensure all clamps and fittings are tight. Hoses can crack over time. They can also loosen from vibrations. Look for any visible damage on the hose material. Even a small pinhole can cause enough water to trigger the LC code.

### Inspect the Sump Area and Filter

The sump is at the bottom of the dishwasher tub. It collects water before draining. The filter is usually in this area. A clogged filter can cause water to back up. This backup might overflow into the base pan. Clear the filter regularly.

Remove the lower spray arm and the filter assembly. Check for food particles, glass shards, or other debris. Clean the filter under running water. Ensure the sump area itself is free of obstructions. A blockage here can lead to improper draining and overflow, triggering the LC error. Make sure you reassemble everything correctly.

## Addressing Water in the Overflow Pan

After finding a leak, you must deal with any water in the overflow pan. This pan is under your dishwasher. It contains the leak sensor. Even a small amount of water will keep the LC code active. The sensor needs to be completely dry. This step is critical to successfully clear the LC code.

To access the overflow pan, you might need to carefully tilt the dishwasher. Some models allow you to pull it out and lean it back. Other models require removal of the lower access panel. Place towels on the floor first to catch any spills. Gently tilt the dishwasher forward or to one side. This allows the accumulated water to drain from the pan.

Use towels or a sponge to soak up all remaining water. Ensure the pan is thoroughly dry. The sensor must be dry to clear the code. You might need to use a fan to speed up drying. Let the pan air dry for a few hours if possible. Do not forget this step. A wet pan will immediately reactivate the LC code. This is a common part of [clearing water from the bottom of a dishwasher](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-clear-water-from-bottom-of-dishwasher).

## Troubleshooting the Drain System

A faulty drain system can also cause the LC code. If water does not drain properly, it can back up. This backed-up water might overflow the tub. Then it drips into the base pan, triggering the sensor. I always check the drain system if other leak sources are not obvious.

Start with the drain hose. It connects your dishwasher to the garbage disposal or a dedicated drain. Check for kinks in the hose. These kinks can block water flow. Ensure the hose has a proper high loop. This prevents dirty water from flowing back into the dishwasher. A low loop can cause back-siphoning and potential overflows.

Next, inspect the connection to the garbage disposal. Make sure the knockout plug is removed if your dishwasher is new. If it is an existing connection, check for clogs. Food particles can build up there. You might need to disconnect the hose at the disposal. Clear any blockages you find. A blocked air gap on your sink can also prevent draining. Clean the air gap if you have one. For more comprehensive steps on [how to clear a dishwasher drain line](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-clear-dishwasher-drain-line), refer to our detailed guide. These steps are crucial for ensuring proper water flow and preventing leaks.

## Inspecting the Water Inlet Valve

Sometimes, the LC code comes from a leak that is harder to spot. The water inlet valve can be a culprit. This valve controls the flow of water into your dishwasher. It is usually located at the bottom front or side of the unit. It connects directly to your home's water supply line.

A worn-out or damaged inlet valve can slowly leak. This leak might be small. It might only happen during the fill cycle. But even a tiny drip over time collects in the overflow pan. This triggers the LC code. Look for any mineral deposits or water stains around the valve body. These are signs of a slow leak.

You may need to remove the lower access panel to see the valve. Inspect the connections leading into and out of the valve. Check the valve housing itself for cracks. If you find a leak at the valve, you will need to replace it. This repair requires turning off the main water supply to your house. It also requires disconnecting the dishwasher from power. This task can be more involved. If you are unsure, consider professional help.

## FAQ Section

### Q: Can I use my Samsung dishwasher with an LC code?
A: No, you should not use your Samsung dishwasher if it displays an LC code. The code indicates a detected leak. The dishwasher stops operation as a safety measure. Continuing to use it could worsen the leak. This increases the risk of water damage to your kitchen. Resolve the LC code before running another cycle.

### Q: How do I locate the leak sensor in my Samsung dishwasher?
A: The leak sensor is typically located in the overflow or base pan. This pan sits at the very bottom of the dishwasher, underneath the wash tub. You may need to remove the lower kick plate or carefully tilt the dishwasher to access this area. The sensor is usually a small float switch.

### Q: Why does my LC code keep coming back even after I dry the pan?
A: If the LC code returns, it means the leak source is still active. You might have missed a small leak point during your inspection. Ensure all hoses, connections, and the door gasket are thoroughly checked. The overflow pan might also not be completely dry. Re-inspect for the original source and dry the pan completely.

### Q: Is the LC code always a serious leak?
A: No, the LC code does not always indicate a serious leak. It can trigger from a very small drip or even condensation. However, any leak, regardless of size, needs attention. Even a minor leak can lead to bigger problems or water damage over time. Always investigate and fix the source.

### Q: How long does it take to fix an LC code issue?
A: The time it takes to fix an LC code issue varies. A simple power reset or drying a small spill might take only minutes. Finding and repairing a more persistent leak, like a hose replacement or a clogged drain, could take an hour or more. If a professional is needed, it depends on their schedule and the complexity of the repair.
