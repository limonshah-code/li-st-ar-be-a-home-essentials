## Easily Clean Stoneware for Lasting Kitchen Performance

Stoneware cookware is a favorite in many kitchens. It offers even heat distribution and a natural non-stick surface over time. Keeping it clean helps it perform well and last longer. Many people wonder how to clean stoneware correctly without damaging its unique properties. I remember when I first got my stoneware; cleaning it seemed like a puzzle. Proper cleaning methods make sure your pieces stay ready for delicious meals. This article explains how to clean stoneware thoroughly, from daily maintenance to deep cleaning stubborn stains. We will cover simple steps to follow and common mistakes to avoid. My goal is to make cleaning stoneware clear and easy for you.

### Takeaway

*   Always allow stoneware to cool completely before cleaning.
*   Avoid harsh detergents, soap, and abrasive scrubbers.
*   Use warm water and a plastic scraper for most food residue.
*   Baking soda paste is effective for stubborn stains and odors.
*   Proper seasoning helps with non-stick properties and easier cleaning.

To clean stoneware, let it cool completely. Scrape off loose food with a plastic scraper. Wash with warm water and a brush, avoiding soap for unglazed pieces. For stubborn stains, create a paste using baking soda and water, apply it, let it sit, then scrub and rinse.

## Understanding Your Stoneware: Glazed vs. Unglazed

Before you clean any piece of stoneware, it is helpful to know what kind you own. Stoneware comes in two main types: glazed and unglazed. Each type has different properties that affect how you clean it. Knowing the difference helps you pick the right method. I found this out the hard way with a new piece, and it made me more careful.

Glazed stoneware has a smooth, shiny finish. This glaze acts as a protective layer. It makes the stoneware less porous and easier to wipe clean. Glazed pieces are often colorful and look nice on display. You can usually use a bit of mild dish soap on glazed stoneware without issue. The glaze keeps the soap from soaking into the material.

Unglazed stoneware, on the other hand, feels rougher to the touch. It has a matte finish and is porous. This means it can absorb liquids and oils over time. Many baking stones and pizza stones are unglazed. Unglazed stoneware often develops a "seasoned" surface with use. This seasoning comes from absorbed oils and creates a natural non-stick layer. Because it is porous, using soap on unglazed stoneware can cause it to absorb the soap. This can leave a soapy taste in your food next time you use it. For unglazed stoneware, water and a scraper are usually best. Understanding these differences is the first step to effective stoneware care. It prevents damage and keeps your cookware ready for use.

## Essential Steps for Daily Stoneware Cleaning

Daily cleaning of your stoneware is simple. It helps prevent food from sticking and building up over time. I always make sure to do this right after using my stoneware. This quick routine keeps your pieces in good shape. It avoids the need for heavy scrubbing later on.

First, let your stoneware cool completely. Never put hot stoneware into cold water. This sudden temperature change can cause it to crack or break. This is called thermal shock, and it is a common mistake. Once cool, scrape off any loose food particles. I use a plastic or nylon scraper for this. Metal utensils can scratch the surface. A silicone spatula also works well.

Next, wash the stoneware with warm water. For unglazed stoneware, this is often all you need. You can use a stiff nylon brush or a plastic scouring pad to gently scrub away residue. Avoid using metal sponges or steel wool. These can damage the surface and remove the natural seasoning. For glazed stoneware, you can add a small amount of mild dish soap to the water. Rinse the stoneware thoroughly to remove all food bits. Make sure no soap residue remains, especially on unglazed pieces. After washing, dry the stoneware completely with a towel or let it air dry. Ensuring it is dry prevents mold or mildew growth. Proper daily cleaning makes deeper cleans less frequent. For Pampered Chef stoneware, which is often unglazed, these daily steps are very important to maintain its seasoned surface. ## Tackling Stubborn Stains and Baked-On Food

Sometimes, food sticks to stoneware, creating stubborn stains. This happens, even with regular use. Do not worry; there are effective ways to remove these without harsh chemicals. I have faced many tough stains, and these methods always work for me. They help bring my stoneware back to a clean state.

For baked-on food, start by soaking the cooled stoneware. Fill it with warm water. Let it sit for a few hours or overnight. This softens the hardened food bits. After soaking, gently scrape the food away with a plastic scraper. You might be surprised how much comes off easily. If the food remains, move to the next step.

Baking soda is your best friend for tough stains and baked-on food. It is a natural abrasive and deodorizer. Make a thick paste using baking soda and a little water. Spread this paste over the stained areas. Let the paste sit on the stoneware for 15-30 minutes. For very stubborn spots, you can leave it longer, even overnight. After the paste has sat, scrub the area with a nylon brush or a non-abrasive scouring pad. The baking soda helps lift the grime. Rinse the stoneware thoroughly with warm water. Repeat if necessary until the stain is gone. This method is effective for dark spots or burnt-on residue. It is safe for both glazed and unglazed stoneware. It helps avoid damage while cleaning deep into the surface. ## Deep Cleaning and Odor Removal for Stoneware

Over time, stoneware can absorb odors or develop a dull appearance. This is especially true for unglazed pieces that absorb cooking oils. A deep cleaning helps refresh your stoneware and remove any lingering smells. I do this a few times a year, or whenever I notice an off odor. It helps keep my stoneware smelling fresh.

For deep cleaning, baking soda is again the star. Create a paste with baking soda and water, but make it a bit thinner than for spot cleaning. Spread a thin layer of this paste over the entire cooking surface of your stoneware. Make sure to cover all areas, even those that look clean. Let this paste sit for at least an hour. For strong odors or heavy discoloration, leave it overnight. The baking soda works to absorb odors and lift embedded grime.

After the paste has done its work, scrub the entire surface. Use a stiff nylon brush or a plastic scrubber. Apply gentle pressure in small circular motions. You will see the paste turn brownish as it picks up residue. Rinse the stoneware very well with warm water. Make sure all baking soda residue is gone. For odor removal, if baking soda alone is not enough, you can try placing the clean, dry stoneware in a cool oven. Turn the oven to its lowest setting (around 200-250°F) for an hour. This low heat can help burn off remaining odors. Always let the stoneware cool completely in the oven before removing. This deep cleaning process restores your stoneware's natural look and smell. It prepares it for many more delicious meals.

## The Role of Seasoning in Stoneware Cleaning

Seasoning is a special process for unglazed stoneware. It involves baking oil into the porous surface. This creates a natural non-stick layer. This layer makes stoneware easier to clean in the long run. Many people do not realize how important seasoning is. I learned its value early on; it makes a huge difference.

When you use unglazed stoneware, cooking oils get absorbed. Over time, these oils polymerize and create a dark, smooth finish. This is the seasoning. It fills the tiny pores in the stoneware. This seasoned layer makes food less likely to stick. It also makes cleanup quicker. When your stoneware is well-seasoned, food residue often scrapes off with ease. You do not need harsh scrubbing or detergents. The seasoning also helps to protect the stoneware itself. It keeps moisture from soaking in too much.

To maintain seasoning, avoid soap on unglazed stoneware. Soap can break down the seasoned layer. This leaves your stoneware less non-stick. It also makes it more prone to sticking again. After cleaning with water and a scraper, make sure to dry the stoneware completely. You can also re-season your stoneware if it looks dull or food starts to stick more often. To re-season, apply a thin layer of cooking oil (like vegetable or canola oil) to the dry surface. Wipe off any excess so it does not feel greasy. Then, bake the stoneware upside down in a preheated oven at 400-450°F for about 30-60 minutes. Let it cool completely in the oven. This process helps build up and maintain that crucial non-stick surface. Properly seasoned stoneware is a joy to cook with and a breeze to clean. ## Common Mistakes to Avoid When Cleaning Stoneware

Cleaning stoneware seems simple, but it is easy to make mistakes. These mistakes can damage your cookware or shorten its life. I made some of these errors when I first started using stoneware. Learning what not to do is as important as knowing what to do. Avoiding these pitfalls keeps your stoneware performing its best.

One major mistake is using soap on unglazed stoneware. As I mentioned, soap can soak into the porous surface. This leaves a soapy taste in your food. It also strips away the valuable seasoning layer. Stick to warm water and a scraper for unglazed pieces. Another common error is using abrasive cleaners or metal scrubbers. Steel wool, harsh scouring pads, and strong chemical cleaners can scratch the surface of both glazed and unglazed stoneware. They can also remove the seasoning or damage the glaze. Always opt for plastic scrapers, nylon brushes, or baking soda paste.

Putting hot stoneware directly into cold water is a big no-no. This causes thermal shock. The sudden temperature change can lead to cracks or breaks. Always let your stoneware cool completely before washing it. Also, do not put wet stoneware into a hot oven or use it when it is still damp. Moisture trapped inside can expand rapidly when heated. This can cause cracking. Always ensure your stoneware is fully dry before storing or reheating it. Lastly, never put stoneware in the dishwasher unless the manufacturer specifically states it is dishwasher safe. Dishwashers use harsh detergents and high heat. These can strip seasoning, damage glaze, or lead to cracks over time. Hand washing is always the safest bet for stoneware. Avoiding these mistakes ensures your stoneware stays in excellent condition for many years.

## Advanced Techniques for Tough Stoneware Challenges

Even with the best care, sometimes stoneware presents unique cleaning challenges. These might include very deep stains or baked-on residue that resists standard methods. These advanced techniques are for those really tough spots. I have used them successfully when other methods fell short.

For extremely stubborn, burnt-on food, consider a "baking soda soak and bake." After trying the paste, if the stain persists, fill the stoneware with water. Add several tablespoons of baking soda to the water. Place the stoneware in a cool oven. Heat the oven to 200°F (95°C) and let it bake for about an hour. The low heat helps loosen the residue. Turn off the oven and let the stoneware cool completely in the oven. Once cool, the burnt-on bits should be much easier to scrape off with a plastic scraper. Rinse well afterwards.

Another option for dark, embedded stains on unglazed stoneware is hydrogen peroxide. Mix hydrogen peroxide with baking soda to form a thick paste. Apply this paste to the stain. Let it sit for several hours or overnight. Hydrogen peroxide has bleaching properties that can lighten deep stains. Always rinse thoroughly multiple times after using hydrogen peroxide. This ensures no residue remains. Always test this method on a small, hidden area first to ensure it does not damage your specific piece. Remember to dry the stoneware completely after any deep cleaning. Proper drying is crucial. ## Caring for Glazed vs. Unglazed Stoneware: Specific Tips

While both types of stoneware are durable, their specific care needs differ. Understanding these nuances helps extend the life of your pieces. I treat my glazed and unglazed stoneware quite differently. This helps them both last a long time.

**For Glazed Stoneware:**
Glazed stoneware is less porous due to its protective coating. This makes it more resistant to stains and easier to clean with mild soap.
*   **Soap Use:** You can use a small amount of mild dish soap and warm water. The glaze prevents soap from soaking in.
*   **Scrubbing:** A soft sponge or nylon brush is usually enough. For tougher spots, a non-abrasive plastic scrubber works well. Avoid metal scrubbers which can scratch the glaze.
*   **Stain Removal:** Most stains wipe off easily. For baked-on food, soaking in warm water usually loosens it. If stains persist, a baking soda paste is safe and effective.
*   **Dishwasher Safety:** Some glazed stoneware is dishwasher safe, but always check the manufacturer's instructions. Hand washing is often preferred to preserve the finish over time.

**For Unglazed Stoneware:**
Unglazed stoneware is porous and develops a natural seasoning. This seasoning is key to its non-stick properties.
*   **No Soap:** Absolutely avoid using soap. It strips the seasoning and can leave a soapy taste. Use only warm water and a plastic scraper or stiff brush.
*   **Seasoning Maintenance:** The natural darkening and oil absorption improve its performance. Do not try to scrub this off. If food sticks, it may need more seasoning.
*   **Deep Cleaning:** Use baking soda paste with water for tough stains and odors. This cleans without stripping the seasoning. Rinse very thoroughly.
*   **Drying:** Ensure it is completely dry before storing to prevent mildew or mold. You can air dry or dry in a low oven.
*   **Pampered Chef Stoneware:** Many Pampered Chef products are unglazed. The cleaning principles discussed for unglazed stoneware apply directly. Their specific instructions often emphasize no soap and building seasoning. For more details on this popular brand, you can read our guide on [how to clean Pampered Chef stoneware](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-clean-pampered-chef-stoneware).

By tailoring your cleaning approach to the specific type of stoneware, you ensure its longevity and continued excellent performance in your kitchen.

## Maintaining Stoneware: Beyond Just Cleaning

Keeping your stoneware in top condition goes beyond just washing it after use. Proper storage and careful handling also play a big part. Thinking about long-term care helps your stoneware last for many years. I have found that a little extra care pays off.

Once your stoneware is clean and completely dry, store it properly. For unglazed pieces, ensure they are bone dry to prevent mildew. Store them in a well-ventilated area. Avoid stacking unglazed stoneware directly on top of each other if possible. If you must stack, place a cloth or paper towel between pieces. This prevents scratches and moisture trapping. Glazed stoneware is more forgiving but still benefits from careful stacking.

Handle stoneware with care. It is durable but can break if dropped or subjected to sudden impacts. Avoid extreme temperature changes, as discussed earlier. Always let it cool slowly after cooking. Never place a hot piece of stoneware on a cold countertop. Use trivets or cooling racks. If you use your stoneware in the oven, place it in a cool oven and let it heat up with the oven. This prevents thermal shock. For similar reasons, when cleaning, be mindful of temperature. Proper care means your stoneware will continue to be a reliable part of your cooking for a long time. It will keep its natural, non-stick properties.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Cleaning Stoneware

### Can I use soap on my stoneware?
You should avoid using soap on unglazed stoneware. Unglazed stoneware is porous and will absorb the soap, which can lead to a soapy taste in your food. Soap also strips away the natural seasoning. For glazed stoneware, a small amount of mild dish soap is usually fine because the glaze acts as a protective barrier. Always rinse thoroughly.

### How do I get rid of black spots or burnt-on food on stoneware?
For black spots or burnt-on food, let the stoneware cool completely. Scrape off loose bits with a plastic scraper. Then, make a thick paste with baking soda and water. Apply it to the stained area and let it sit for at least 15-30 minutes, or even overnight for tough stains. Scrub with a nylon brush and rinse thoroughly.

### Is stoneware dishwasher safe?
Generally, no. Most stoneware, especially unglazed types, is not dishwasher safe. Dishwashers use harsh detergents and high heat which can strip the seasoning from unglazed stoneware or damage the glaze and lead to cracks on glazed pieces. Hand washing with warm water is always the safest and recommended method for cleaning stoneware.

### Why is my stoneware sticky after cleaning?
If your unglazed stoneware feels sticky, it might be due to excess oil from seasoning that has not fully polymerized, or residue from certain foods. Try re-baking it at a high temperature (around 450°F) for 30-60 minutes after wiping off any visible excess oil. This helps polymerize the oil and create a smooth, non-sticky surface.

### How often should I season my unglazed stoneware?
Unglazed stoneware naturally seasons with regular use as it absorbs cooking oils. You do not need to actively season it every time. If your stoneware starts to look dull, or food begins to stick more often, then it is a good time to re-season it. A light oil application followed by baking is sufficient.

### Can I use steel wool or abrasive pads on stoneware?
No, you should never use steel wool or abrasive metal pads on stoneware. These can scratch the surface of glazed stoneware, damaging the finish. For unglazed stoneware, they can remove the valuable seasoning layer. Always use plastic scrapers, nylon brushes, or non-abrasive scrubbing pads.
