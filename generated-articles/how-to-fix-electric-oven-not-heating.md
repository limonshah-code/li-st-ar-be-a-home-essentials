### Takeaway

*   Always disconnect power before starting any oven repair.
*   Check the heating elements for visible damage first.
*   Test the temperature sensor and thermal fuse for continuity.
*   Confirm the oven has proper power supply.
*   Consider professional help for control board issues.

To fix an electric oven not heating, disconnect power, then check heating elements for breaks. Test the temperature sensor and thermal fuse with a multimeter for continuity. Replace any parts that show no continuity. Also, confirm the oven receives power from the circuit breaker.

## Prioritizing Safety Before Any Oven Repair

Before you start any repair on your electric oven, safety is most important. Electricity can cause serious harm. You must protect yourself from electric shock. Always disconnect the power to your oven first.

Turn off the circuit breaker for the oven. This breaker is usually in your home's electrical panel. It might be labeled "Oven" or "Range." If unsure, turn off the main breaker to your entire house. Confirm the power is off by trying to turn the oven on. No lights or sounds mean the power is off. Wear insulated gloves for extra protection. Use non-conductive tools whenever possible. Make sure your work area is dry. Water conducts electricity. Keep children and pets away from the work area. This prevents accidents during the repair process. Do not rush any steps. Taking your time reduces the chance of mistakes. Safety ensures you can fix the problem without harm. Remember, your well-being comes first.

## Checking for Power Supply Issues

When your electric oven does not heat, the power supply is the first thing to check. No power means no heat. Start by looking at your circuit breaker panel. A tripped breaker looks different from an "on" one. It often sits in the middle position.

Reset the breaker by flipping it completely off, then back to the "on" position. Sometimes, a loose plug can cause power problems. Ensure the oven's power cord is fully plugged into the wall outlet. If your oven connects directly to the house wiring, check for any loose wires at the terminal block. A multimeter can test the outlet for proper voltage. Set the multimeter to measure AC voltage. Insert the probes into the outlet slots. A reading of around 240 volts means the outlet is working. If you get no reading or a low reading, the problem is with your home's electrical system, not the oven itself. In such cases, contact a licensed electrician. Do not try to fix wiring you are unsure about. Electrical work requires specific knowledge and tools. Ensuring a stable power supply is a basic step in oven troubleshooting. A faulty power connection can be a simple reason [why your electric oven is not heating up](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-fix-electric-oven-that-wont-heat-up).

## Inspecting the Heating Elements for Damage

After confirming power, inspect the oven's heating elements. These coils glow red when working correctly. A broken element will not heat. Electric ovens typically have two main elements: the bake element at the bottom and the broil element at the top.

First, visually check both elements. Look for any visible breaks, cracks, or blisters on the element's surface. If an element is burnt out, it might show signs of charring or a clear break. A broken element means no heat or uneven heating. If you see visible damage, the element needs replacement. This is a common part to fail. You can also test the element's continuity using a multimeter. Disconnect the oven's power first. Pull the oven away from the wall to access the back panel. Remove the screws holding the element in place. Carefully pull the element out. Disconnect its wires. Set your multimeter to the ohms (resistance) setting. Touch one probe to each terminal of the element. A healthy element will show a low resistance reading, typically between 10 to 50 ohms. If the multimeter shows "OL" (open circuit) or an extremely high reading, the element is faulty. You will need to get a new element. Replacing a heating element is a common DIY fix for a non-heating oven. We have a detailed guide on [how to replace electric oven heating element](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-replace-electric-oven-heating-element).

## Testing the Oven Temperature Sensor

The oven temperature sensor tells the control board the current oven temperature. If this sensor is faulty, the oven cannot regulate heat. It might not heat at all or heat improperly. The sensor is usually a small metal probe located inside the oven cavity, often near the top rear.

To test the temperature sensor, first, disconnect the oven from power. Locate the sensor. It typically has two wires connected to it. Carefully pull the sensor out. You might need to unscrew it. Once removed, disconnect the wires. Use a multimeter set to measure resistance (ohms). Touch one probe to each terminal of the sensor. A working sensor will show a specific resistance value at room temperature, usually around 1090 ohms at 70 degrees Fahrenheit (21 degrees Celsius). This value varies slightly by brand and model. You can often find the exact resistance specification in your oven's service manual. If the reading is far off or shows "OL" (open circuit), the sensor is bad.

A common test is to measure its resistance, then place the sensor in a cup of ice water. The resistance should change significantly. If the resistance does not change, or if it reads as an open circuit, the sensor is faulty. A faulty sensor sends incorrect signals. This causes the heating elements not to turn on. Replacing a temperature sensor is usually a straightforward repair. It can bring your oven back to life if this is the cause.

## Examining the Thermal Fuse

The thermal fuse is a safety device. It prevents the oven from overheating. If the oven gets too hot, the fuse blows, cutting power to the heating circuit. This is to prevent a fire hazard. Once a thermal fuse blows, it must be replaced. It is not resettable.

The thermal fuse is usually located on the back of the oven, often near the control panel or elements. You will need to pull the oven away from the wall to access it. Disconnect the power before you begin. The fuse looks like a small cylinder with two wires connected. It might be wrapped in insulation. Once you find it, disconnect the wires. Use a multimeter set to measure continuity. Place one probe on each terminal of the fuse. A working fuse will show continuity, meaning the multimeter will beep or show a reading close to zero ohms. If the multimeter shows "OL" (open circuit) or no reading, the thermal fuse is blown. This means it is the reason your oven is not heating.

If you find a blown thermal fuse, it is important to also investigate why it blew. An underlying issue, like a faulty cooling fan or a short circuit, could have caused the oven to overheat. Simply replacing the fuse without addressing the root cause might lead to the new fuse blowing quickly. Consult your oven's wiring diagram if you are unsure about the fuse's location. This critical safety component is often overlooked but can be a direct cause of a completely dead oven.

## Diagnosing a Faulty Control Board

The control board is the brain of your electric oven. It sends signals to the heating elements, temperature sensor, and other components. If the control board malfunctions, the oven will not heat. It might also show error codes or behave erratically. Diagnosing a faulty control board can be more challenging.

First, check for any visible signs of damage on the board. Look for burnt spots, melted components, or bulging capacitors. These are clear indicators of a problem. However, internal failures are not always visible. If other components like the elements, sensor, and thermal fuse test good, the control board is a strong suspect. A professional appliance technician often has specialized tools to test control boards. They can also read specific error codes. For instance, if you have an LG oven and it's not heating, issues with its specific control board might be more common, and you might find more information on [why is my LG oven not heating up](https://homeessentialsguide.com/why-is-my-lg-oven-not-heating-up).

Replacing a control board is typically a more expensive repair. It also requires careful handling of electronic components. Make sure the new board is the exact part number for your oven model. Installing the wrong board can cause further damage. If you are not comfortable working with circuit boards, it is best to call a qualified technician. They can diagnose the problem accurately and replace the board correctly. Sometimes, replacing the control board is the only solution when all other components check out.

## Checking the Oven's Selector Switch or Thermostat

The selector switch, often called the function switch, directs power to different oven functions like bake, broil, or self-clean. If this switch is faulty, it might not send power to the heating elements in the chosen mode. Similarly, the oven thermostat (separate from the temperature sensor) mechanically controls the heating cycle in older models. A broken thermostat will not allow the oven to heat up or maintain temperature.

To test the selector switch, you need a multimeter. Disconnect power to the oven. Remove the oven's control panel to access the switch. Look for wires connected to the switch. With the multimeter set to continuity mode, test each terminal pair as you turn the switch to different settings. Refer to your oven's wiring diagram to know which terminals should have continuity in each setting. If a specific setting, like "Bake," shows no continuity when it should, the switch is faulty. For a thermostat, test for continuity across its terminals at different temperature settings. If it shows no continuity or does not change as you adjust temperature, it is likely broken. Replacing these parts involves disconnecting many wires. Label them clearly before removal. This ensures correct reassembly. These components are vital for the oven's heating functions. A fault here often means the oven gets no signal to begin heating. This step is crucial for older oven models, especially when your electric oven is not heating at all.

## FAQ Section

### Why would my electric oven suddenly stop heating?

An electric oven can suddenly stop heating due to several reasons. Common causes include a blown thermal fuse, a failed heating element (bake or broil), or a malfunctioning temperature sensor. A power supply issue, such as a tripped circuit breaker, can also stop the oven from heating.

### How do I reset my electric oven when it's not heating?

You can try resetting your electric oven by turning off its circuit breaker for 5-10 minutes. Then, turn the breaker back on. This can sometimes clear minor electronic glitches. If the oven still does not heat, a more specific component failure likely exists.

### Can a broken heating element be fixed or does it need to be replaced?

A broken heating element cannot be fixed. If the element is visibly broken, cracked, or charred, it must be replaced. Heating elements are designed as single units. Once they fail internally, they do not conduct electricity properly.

### How much does it cost to fix an electric oven that is not heating?

The cost to fix an electric oven varies. It depends on the faulty part and labor rates. Replacing a heating element might cost $100-$250. A temperature sensor costs less, perhaps $50-$150. A control board replacement can be expensive, ranging from $200-$600 or more, including parts and labor.

### Is it worth fixing an old electric oven that's not heating?

Whether to fix an old electric oven depends on its age, condition, and repair cost. If the repair cost is more than half the price of a new, comparable oven, consider replacing it. Older ovens might have other parts fail soon. Evaluate the total value and potential future repairs.

### What are the signs of a faulty oven temperature sensor?

Signs of a faulty oven temperature sensor include the oven not heating to the set temperature, heating unevenly, or not heating at all. The oven might also display error codes. Sometimes, the oven preheats slowly or cycles on and off too frequently.
