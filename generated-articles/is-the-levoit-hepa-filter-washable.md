### The Science Behind Non-Washable HEPA Filters

The effectiveness of non-washable HEPA filters stems from their unique design and the physics of particle capture. These filters are not simply screens. They are intricate webs of very fine fibers. These fibers are typically glass fibers or synthetic polymer fibers. They are packed tightly together. Air flows through this labyrinth. Particles get trapped through several mechanisms.

One mechanism is **interception**. Particles follow the air streamline. If a particle's path comes within one fiber radius of a fiber, it is intercepted and captured. Another mechanism is **impaction**. Larger, heavier particles cannot follow the air streamlines around a fiber. Their inertia carries them into direct contact with the fiber. They then stick to it. The third critical mechanism is **diffusion**. This applies to the smallest particles (0.1 microns and below). These particles move randomly due to Brownian motion. This erratic movement increases their chances of colliding with and sticking to filter fibers.

Water disrupts all these mechanisms. When wet, the delicate fibers clump together. This opens up larger pathways in the filter. It reduces the surface area available for trapping particles. Water can also cause some filter materials to swell or degrade. This permanently alters their structure. Furthermore, the electrostatic charge, which helps attract tiny particles, can be neutralized by water. Once these filters are wet, they cannot return to their original, highly efficient state. This is why washing a true HEPA filter is never recommended. It effectively destroys its filtration capabilities.
