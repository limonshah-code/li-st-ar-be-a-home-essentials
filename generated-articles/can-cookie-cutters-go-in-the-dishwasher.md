## Can Cookie Cutters Go In The Dishwasher? Get the Facts

Baking delicious cookies brings much joy. Cleaning up afterward sometimes feels like a chore. You finish shaping and baking, then face a pile of tools. One common question often comes to mind: **Can cookie cutters go in the dishwasher**? This question pops up for many home bakers. I want to tell you about the best way to clean these tools. It is important to know which cookie cutters can survive a dishwasher cycle and which need hand washing.

This article will explain the different types of cookie cutter materials. We will look at how each material reacts to the dishwasher. I will share tips for keeping your cookie cutters in good shape. You will learn about preventing damage like rust or warping. Proper cleaning means your cookie cutters last for many years. Let's find out how to keep your baking tools ready for the next batch of treats.

### Takeaway:

*   **Check Material First:** Metal (especially tinplate) can rust; plastic can warp; wood must be hand-washed.
*   **Hand Wash for Safety:** Most cookie cutters benefit from gentle hand washing to preserve their shape and finish.
*   **Dishwasher Use (Limited):** Stainless steel and sturdy, BPA-free plastic may go on the top rack with care.
*   **Dry Thoroughly:** Always dry cookie cutters completely to prevent rust and mold.

### Clear Answer to the Main Query:

No, not all cookie cutters can go in the dishwasher. Dishwasher safety depends on the material. Stainless steel and certain durable plastics often tolerate dishwashers well, usually on the top rack. Tinplate metal, wood, and some fragile plastics require hand washing to prevent rust, warping, or damage. Always check the manufacturer's guidelines if available.

### Understanding Cookie Cutter Materials and Dishwasher Compatibility

Cookie cutters come in many materials. Each material reacts differently to heat and strong detergents. Understanding these differences helps you decide if your cookie cutters can go in the dishwasher. Incorrect cleaning can shorten the life of your baking tools. Knowing the material is the first step to proper care.

Most metal cookie cutters are tinplate steel or stainless steel. Tinplate steel is prone to rust. Stainless steel resists rust better. Plastic cookie cutters are common and come in various types. Some plastics handle heat well, others do not. Wooden cookie cutters are natural and need gentle care. Silicone cookie cutters are flexible and generally heat-resistant. Each material needs a specific cleaning approach. It ensures they stay in good condition for future baking.

*   **Tinplate Steel:** These are often silver-colored and lightweight. They are very common. Tinplate rusts easily when exposed to water for too long. The dishwasher's hot water and harsh detergents are not good for tinplate. Water can get into small seams. This makes rust appear quickly. It is better to avoid putting these in the dishwasher.
*   **Stainless Steel:** Stainless steel cookie cutters are also metal. They are shinier and feel more solid. This material resists rust much better than tinplate. Many stainless steel items are dishwasher safe. You can usually place these in the dishwasher. However, ensure they dry fully to avoid water spots.
*   **Plastic:** Plastic cookie cutters are popular for their colors and shapes. The safety of plastic in a dishwasher varies greatly. Some high-quality plastics are dishwasher safe. Lower quality plastics can warp or melt from the heat. Check for a "dishwasher safe" label.
*   **Wood:** Wooden cookie cutters are often decorative or traditional. Wood is a natural material. It absorbs water. A dishwasher's hot water and drying cycle can cause wood to crack or split. Wood can also grow mold if it does not dry properly. You should never put wooden cookie cutters in the dishwasher.
*   **Silicone:** Silicone is flexible and heat-resistant. Silicone cookie cutters are almost always dishwasher safe. They can withstand high temperatures. Place them on the top rack of your dishwasher. They are easy to clean and maintain.

I always check the material first. This simple step saves me from ruining a favorite cookie cutter. Proper care means more cookie baking fun.

### Metal Cookie Cutters: To Wash or Not to Wash?

Metal cookie cutters are a staple in most kitchens. They are great for cutting shapes precisely. The main concern with metal cookie cutters is rust. Rust can happen quickly if you are not careful. My experience tells me that rust is the biggest enemy of metal cutters.

There are two main types of metal cookie cutters: tinplate and stainless steel. Tinplate cookie cutters are generally not dishwasher safe. The protective tin coating can wear off in the dishwasher. This exposes the steel underneath to moisture. Once the steel is exposed, rust spots appear fast. I once put a beloved tinplate cutter in the dishwasher, and it came out with orange rust. It was a sad lesson. For tinplate, hand washing is always the better choice. You can learn more about caring for metal baking items by reading about [how to clean bakeware cookie sheets](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-clean-bakeware-cookie-sheets).

Stainless steel cookie cutters are much more durable. They are made to resist rust and corrosion. Most stainless steel cookie cutters are dishwasher safe. I often place my stainless steel cutters on the top rack of the dishwasher. The top rack usually has less intense heat. This protects them from extreme conditions. Even with stainless steel, it is important to dry them completely. Water left on them can still cause water spots or even mild discoloration over time. Rust, though rare for stainless steel, can still occur if the material is low quality or if the surface gets scratched badly.

When cleaning metal cookie cutters, especially tinplate, gentle handling is key. I use warm, soapy water and a soft sponge. I scrub off any dough bits carefully. After washing, I dry them right away with a towel. Air drying is not enough for tinplate. This prevents water from sitting on the surface. Drying them by hand ensures no rust forms. For tough, baked-on dough, soaking helps. You can soak them briefly in warm water. Then, scrub them gently. If you want more tips on stubborn residue, you might find ideas in articles on [how to clean baked on cookie sheets](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-clean-baked-on-cookie-sheets).

Always consider the type of metal you have. If you are unsure, choose hand washing. It is the safest way to keep your metal cookie cutters in good condition for many years of baking.

### Plastic Cookie Cutters in the Dishwasher: A Safe Bet?

Plastic cookie cutters are popular because they are colorful and affordable. They come in many fun shapes. When it comes to dishwashers, plastic cookie cutters are a mixed bag. Some are fine, others are not. I have learned to check them closely before tossing them in.

The main issue with plastic in the dishwasher is heat. Dishwashers use hot water and hot drying cycles. This heat can cause certain plastics to warp or melt. A warped cookie cutter will not cut a clean shape. It can become useless. Lower quality plastics or those not marked "dishwasher safe" are most at risk. High heat can also cause some plastics to release chemicals. This is especially true if they are not BPA-free. Always look for a "dishwasher safe" label. If you do not see this label, hand washing is a safer choice.

When using the dishwasher for plastic cookie cutters, always place them on the top rack. The top rack is further from the heating element. It receives less intense heat than the bottom rack. This reduces the risk of warping. Use a gentle cycle if your dishwasher has one. Avoid the "sanitize" or "heavy duty" cycles for plastics. These cycles use higher temperatures. I usually turn off the heated dry cycle for plastic items. I let them air dry instead. This small step protects them from excessive heat exposure. You can get more familiar with different dishwasher functions by reading [how to use dishwasher](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-use-dishwasher) guides.

Another concern with plastic is staining. Some food dyes or strong spices can stain plastic. This is more common with lighter-colored plastics. Dishwasher detergents might not always prevent these stains. If a plastic cookie cutter already has a stain, the dishwasher may set it further. For highly pigmented doughs, I often hand wash plastic cutters immediately. This prevents stains from setting.

In summary, plastic cookie cutters can be dishwasher safe. You must know the type of plastic. Always use the top rack and avoid high heat drying. If in doubt, a quick hand wash with warm, soapy water is the best way to keep them looking good and working well.

### Wooden and Silicone Cookie Cutters: Special Considerations

Beyond metal and plastic, you might have wooden or silicone cookie cutters. These materials have unique properties. They need different care routines. My experience shows that these materials often require special attention.

Wooden cookie cutters are charming and traditional. However, wood and dishwashers do not mix. Wood is a natural material. It absorbs water. The hot water and strong detergents in a dishwasher can strip natural oils from the wood. This makes the wood dry out. It can cause cracking or splitting. Repeated wetting and drying cycles also encourage mold growth. Mold can hide in crevices. This makes the cookie cutter unsafe for food use. I always hand wash my wooden cookie cutters. I use mild soap and warm water. After washing, I dry them immediately and thoroughly with a towel. I let them air dry completely before storing them. Sometimes, I rub a little food-grade mineral oil on them. This helps to condition the wood and keeps it from drying out.

Silicone cookie cutters are very flexible and durable. They are made from a type of rubber. Silicone handles high temperatures very well. Most silicone cookie cutters are dishwasher safe. They can go on either the top or bottom rack. I usually put them on the top rack with other smaller items. Silicone does not rust, warp, or crack in the dishwasher. It is a very convenient material for baking tools.

One thing to watch with silicone is residue. Sometimes, food oils or bits of dough can stick to silicone. The dishwasher usually cleans them well. If you notice any greasy feel, a quick scrub with dish soap helps. Silicone can also sometimes absorb odors. If your silicone cutter starts to smell like strong spices or detergents, soak it in a vinegar solution. Then, wash it again. Silicone also can pick up lint or dust if left in the open. Storing them in a clean drawer helps keep them ready to use.

Wooden and silicone cookie cutters offer different benefits. Knowing how to care for each type ensures they last a long time. Hand washing is key for wood. Dishwashing is fine for silicone. This simple rule makes kitchen cleanup easier.

### The Risks of Dishwashing Delicate Cookie Cutters

Putting delicate cookie cutters in the dishwasher carries risks. Not all baking tools are built for the harsh environment of a dishwasher. I learned this the hard way with a few treasured items. Understanding these risks helps you protect your cookie cutters.

One major risk is physical damage. The strong jets of water inside a dishwasher can toss around small, lightweight items. Cookie cutters can hit against other dishes or the dishwasher racks. This can bend or dent soft metal cutters. It can also chip or break plastic ones. Intricate designs, especially, can suffer damage. Thin edges or small details might get bent out of shape. Once a cookie cutter is bent, it does not cut dough cleanly. Its original shape is lost.

Rust is another significant danger, particularly for tinplate metal cookie cutters. As I mentioned before, tinplate steel lacks strong rust resistance. The dishwasher's long exposure to hot, soapy water, followed by a hot drying cycle, strips away any protection. This process accelerates oxidation. Rust spots appear quickly. These spots can stain your dough. They make the cookie cutter unhygienic. If you see rust, it is best to discard the cutter. You can avoid this by hand washing and thoroughly drying tinplate items. You might find more information on preventing rust in various bakeware by checking [how to clean old cookie sheets](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-clean-old-cookie-sheets).

High heat from the dishwasher can also cause problems. Plastic cookie cutters can warp or melt. Even if they do not completely melt, they might soften and lose their shape. This makes them useless for cutting. Hot water can also dull the finish of certain metals or plastics. This makes them look old and worn out sooner. For painted or decorated cookie cutters, the high heat and strong detergents can strip off the design. This ruins their aesthetic appeal.

Finally, cross-contamination is a concern if the dishwasher itself is not clean. Food particles from other dishes can get onto your cookie cutters. If your dishwasher needs cleaning, dirty water can circulate. I make sure my dishwasher is clean. I clean the filter regularly. You can learn more about keeping your appliance clean by checking out [how often should you clean dishwasher](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-often-should-you-clean-dishwasher). To avoid all these risks, especially for your favorite or most delicate cookie cutters, hand washing is always the safest method. It gives you control over the cleaning process.

### Best Practices for Hand-Washing Cookie Cutters

Hand washing is often the best method for cleaning cookie cutters. It provides control and gentle care. This helps preserve your baking tools. I find that hand washing makes them last longer. It is not a difficult process.

Here is a simple, step-by-step guide for hand washing:

1.  **Rinse Immediately:** After using your cookie cutters, rinse them under warm running water. This removes most of the leftover dough. Dough bits are easier to clean when wet. Do not let dough dry onto the cutters.
2.  **Prepare Soapy Water:** Fill a sink or a basin with warm water. Add a small amount of mild dish soap. I use a gentle, unscented soap. Strong soaps can be harsh on certain materials.
3.  **Wash Gently:** Place the cookie cutters in the soapy water. Use a soft sponge or a brush to clean them. Pay attention to all edges and crevices. For intricate designs, a small brush (like an old toothbrush) works well. Avoid abrasive scrubbers. These can scratch metal or plastic surfaces.
4.  **Rinse Thoroughly:** Rinse each cookie cutter under clean, warm running water. Make sure to wash off all soap residue. Leftover soap can dull surfaces or leave a taste.
5.  **Dry Immediately and Completely:** This step is crucial, especially for metal cookie cutters. Use a clean, dry towel to dry each cutter by hand. Do not let them air dry for long, particularly tinplate. Water spots and rust can form quickly. For wooden cutters, ensure they are completely dry before storing. You can also place them on a clean rack for a short time to finish air drying, but only after towel drying first.
6.  **Store Properly:** Once dry, store your cookie cutters in a dry place. A container with a lid protects them from dust. It also keeps them from scratching each other.

Hand washing takes a little more time than using a dishwasher. But it prevents many common problems. It helps maintain the quality and appearance of your cookie cutters. This method is especially important for delicate, tinplate, or wooden cutters. I always prefer hand washing for my special cookie cutters. It guarantees they stay in perfect condition. ### Dishwasher Settings and Placement for Cookie Cutters

If you choose to use the dishwasher for your cookie cutters, settings and placement matter. Proper technique can minimize potential damage. I always adjust my approach based on the cookie cutter material.

**Top Rack Placement:**
Always place dishwasher-safe cookie cutters on the top rack. The top rack is generally safer for delicate items. It gets less direct heat than the bottom rack. This reduces the risk of warping for plastic. It also lessens the intensity of water jets. These jets can knock items around. Securing them is important. I often use the small compartments or clips if my dishwasher has them. This stops the cutters from moving.

**Gentle Cycle Selection:**
Choose a gentle or light wash cycle. Avoid heavy-duty or sanitize cycles. These cycles use higher temperatures and stronger water pressure. They are too harsh for most cookie cutters. A delicate cycle still cleans effectively without the extreme conditions. I often select an "eco" or "light" wash setting. It is usually enough to remove cookie dough residue.

**Managing Heat Drying:**
The heated dry cycle is a big enemy for many cookie cutters. High heat can warp plastic. It can also cause tinplate to rust faster. For these reasons, I often turn off the heated dry option. I let the cookie cutters air dry instead. If your dishwasher does not allow turning off the heated dry, remove the cookie cutters before the drying cycle starts. You can then hand dry them immediately. This step is critical for preventing damage.

**Detergent Choice:**
Use a standard, good-quality dishwasher detergent. Avoid harsh, industrial-strength cleansers. These can be too strong for delicate materials. Too much detergent can also leave residue. This residue might affect the look or taste of future cookies. I use the amount of detergent recommended for my dishwasher. Overfilling does not make things cleaner. It can sometimes cause problems.

By following these simple guidelines, you can safely put some cookie cutters in the dishwasher. This applies mostly to stainless steel and sturdy, labeled-safe plastics. Remember, hand washing is always the safest option for any item you are unsure about. Knowing how your dishwasher works helps. ### Preventing Rust and Damage to Metal Cookie Cutters

Metal cookie cutters are durable tools. But they need specific care to prevent rust and other damage. Rust can ruin a cookie cutter. It makes it unusable. I want to share my best tips for keeping metal cutters in top shape.

**Immediate and Thorough Drying:**
This is the most important step for preventing rust. Water is the primary cause of rust. After washing, do not let metal cookie cutters air dry for long. Immediately towel dry each one. Use a clean, absorbent cloth. Pay close attention to seams and small corners. These areas tend to hold water. If you use a dishwasher for stainless steel, remove them quickly after the wash cycle. Then, hand dry them. This stops water spots. It also ensures all moisture is gone.

**Conditioning Tinplate Cutters (Optional but Recommended):**
For tinplate cutters, a thin layer of oil can add protection. After washing and completely drying them, wipe them with a very light coating of food-grade vegetable oil. I use a paper towel with a tiny bit of oil. This creates a barrier against moisture. It is similar to seasoning a cast iron pan. This step is especially helpful if you live in a humid area.

**Proper Storage:**
How you store your metal cookie cutters also matters. Store them in a dry place. A drawer or container is good. Avoid damp areas like under the sink. Stack them carefully to prevent scratching. Scratches can expose raw metal. This makes it more prone to rust. Sometimes, I place a desiccant packet (like the ones found in new shoe boxes) in the storage container. It absorbs moisture. This further protects the cutters.

**Checking for Existing Rust:**
If you find rust spots, you might be able to remove them. For minor spots, a paste of baking soda and water can work. Apply the paste, let it sit, then scrub gently. Rinse and dry immediately. However, if rust is widespread or deep, it is safer to discard the cookie cutter. Rust can be hard to remove completely. It can also be unhygienic.

By taking these steps, you can greatly extend the life of your metal cookie cutters. I follow these rules for my own cutters. They still look good after many years of use. ### When to Choose Hand Washing Over the Dishwasher

While dishwashers offer convenience, hand washing is often the superior choice for many cookie cutters. Knowing when to choose hand washing protects your tools. It extends their life. I make this decision every time I bake.

You should always choose hand washing for:

*   **Tinplate Cookie Cutters:** These metal cutters rust very easily. The dishwasher's hot, wet environment is their worst enemy. Hand washing with warm, soapy water and immediate towel drying prevents rust. This preserves their integrity.
*   **Wooden Cookie Cutters:** Wood absorbs water. Dishwashers cause wood to swell, crack, and potentially grow mold. Hand washing with mild soap and careful drying is essential. It keeps the wood from degrading.
*   **Delicate or Intricate Designs:** Cookie cutters with fine details, sharp edges, or thin structures are prone to damage in the dishwasher. Strong water jets can bend or break them. Hand washing allows you to clean these delicate parts gently.
*   **Painted or Decorated Cookie Cutters:** The high heat and strong detergents in a dishwasher can strip off paint or decorative finishes. Hand washing protects these special designs. It keeps them looking vibrant.
*   **Vintage or Sentimental Cutters:** Old cookie cutters, especially antique ones, are often more fragile. They might be made from materials that do not withstand modern dishwashers. Hand washing ensures these heirlooms stay intact. I hand wash my grandmother's cookie cutters. They mean a lot to me.
*   **Unknown Materials:** If you are unsure what material your cookie cutter is made of, or if it lacks a "dishwasher safe" label, hand wash it. This is the safest default option. It avoids any surprises.

Hand washing gives you control over the cleaning process. You can tailor the water temperature and scrubbing intensity. This personal care ensures that each cookie cutter receives the attention it needs. It helps prevent damage. It also maintains the sharpness of cutting edges. I find that a quick hand wash after baking is not a big chore. It is a worthwhile investment for preserving my collection.
