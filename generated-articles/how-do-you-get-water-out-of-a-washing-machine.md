## How to Get Water Out of a Washing Machine Safely

Waking up to a washing machine full of standing water can be a frustrating experience. This common issue stops your laundry routine cold. You need to get water out of a washing machine to avoid spills and damage. It often indicates a drainage problem. This situation might feel overwhelming at first. However, many solutions exist.

This guide helps you understand why your machine holds water. It provides clear, step-by-step instructions for safe water removal. We will explore various methods, from simple manual draining to clearing stubborn clogs. You will learn about crucial safety precautions and practical troubleshooting tips. We also cover when to seek professional help. Let's get that water out and your laundry back on track.

### Takeaway

When your washing machine is full of water and won't drain, follow these key steps:

*   **Disconnect Power and Water:** Always unplug the machine and turn off water supply valves first.
*   **Manual Drain Hose:** Locate and lower the drain hose into a bucket to remove most of the water.
*   **Clean Pump Filter:** Access and clean the drain pump filter, which often gets clogged.
*   **Inspect Drain Hose:** Check the main drain hose for kinks or blockages.
*   **Call for Help:** Contact a professional technician if the issue persists after these steps.

### Quick Answer: How to Get Water Out of a Washing Machine

To get water out of a washing machine, first disconnect its power and water supply. Then, locate the drain hose at the back. Place the hose end into a bucket lower than the machine. This allows water to drain by gravity. For remaining water, access and clean the drain pump filter. Regularly cleaning this filter helps prevent future drainage issues.

## Understanding Why Your Washing Machine Won't Drain

Your washing machine relies on a functioning drain system. When this system fails, water stays in the drum. Several issues can cause your washing machine to not drain properly. Understanding these problems helps you find the right fix. I have seen many people overlook simple causes.

One common reason is a clogged drain pump filter. This filter catches lint, coins, and other small items. If it gets too full, water cannot pass through. Another frequent culprit is a kinked or blocked drain hose. The hose carries water away from the machine. Any obstruction prevents water flow.

The drain pump itself can fail. This is a mechanical part that pushes water out. A broken pump cannot move water. Issues with the house's drain standpipe can also cause problems. If the standpipe is clogged, water backs up into the machine. Lastly, sometimes a faulty lid switch or control board can prevent the spin cycle and drainage from activating. This means the machine thinks it is not safe to drain. Addressing these common issues helps get your machine working again.

### Common Causes of Drainage Failure

Several parts work together to drain your washing machine. A problem with any of these parts stops the drainage process. I often tell people to check the simplest things first.

*   **Clogged Drain Pump Filter:** This filter is designed to protect the pump. It traps small items and lint. Over time, it can become completely blocked. This prevents water from reaching the pump. Many modern front-load machines have an accessible filter.
*   **Kinked or Blocked Drain Hose:** The drain hose connects the washing machine to a standpipe or sink. If the hose gets bent behind the machine, water cannot flow. Debris can also build up inside the hose. This reduces the hose's diameter and restricts water movement.
*   **Faulty Drain Pump:** The drain pump uses an impeller to push water out. If the impeller breaks or the motor fails, the pump stops working. A buzzing sound with no water movement often signals a pump issue.
*   **Clogged House Drain/Standpipe:** The washing machine drains into your home's plumbing system. If that plumbing is clogged, water backs up. This can cause the machine to overflow or simply not drain. This issue is outside the washing machine itself.
*   **Lid Switch or Door Lock Issues:** For safety, many machines will not spin or drain if the lid or door is open. A faulty switch or lock can make the machine "think" the door is open. This prevents the drain cycle from starting. This is especially true for models that rely on these safety features for proper operation. Sometimes, you can troubleshoot specific brand issues. For example, if you have a Samsung machine and it's not getting water, this could be related to various sensors. ## Prioritizing Safety: Preparing for Water Removal

Safety is the first step before you attempt to get water out of a washing machine. Working with water and electricity can be dangerous. Ignoring safety precautions can lead to electric shock or further damage. I always stress the importance of these initial steps. They protect you and your home.

Begin by disconnecting the machine from its power source. Unplug the washing machine from the wall outlet. This cuts off all electrical supply to the appliance. Do not proceed until you confirm the machine is unplugged. This prevents accidental startup or electrical hazards during your work.

Next, you must turn off the water supply to the washing machine. Look behind the machine for two hoses connected to water faucets. These are usually red for hot water and blue for cold water. Turn both faucets clockwise until they are completely closed. This stops any more water from entering the machine. Knowing [how to turn off water to washing machine](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-turn-off-water-to-washing-machine) is a fundamental skill for any homeowner. You prevent more water from entering the drum during the drainage process.

Gather your tools. You will need buckets or shallow pans to collect the water. Have old towels or rags ready to soak up spills. A mop can also be helpful. Wear rubber gloves to protect your hands. Prepare a clear path to a drain or sink where you can empty the collected water. Proper preparation makes the drainage process much smoother and safer.

### Essential Safety Steps Before Starting

*   **Unplug the Washing Machine:** Always disconnect the power cord from the electrical outlet. This eliminates the risk of electric shock. Never work on an appliance while it is still plugged in.
*   **Turn Off Water Supply:** Locate the hot and cold water supply valves behind the machine. Turn them fully clockwise to shut off the water flow. This prevents additional water from filling the drum. This step is critical before you start working.
*   **Prepare for Water Spills:** Place plenty of old towels and absorbent rags on the floor around the machine. Have several large buckets or shallow pans nearby. These will catch the water as you drain it. A wet-dry vacuum can also be very useful for large spills.
*   **Wear Protective Gear:** Put on rubber gloves to protect your hands. Safety glasses are also a good idea. This protects your eyes from splashing water or debris.
*   **Clear the Area:** Move any obstacles or valuable items away from the washing machine. You will need space to work. Ensure good lighting to see what you are doing. Proper lighting is essential for detailed work, especially in cramped spaces.

## Manual Drainage: The Hose Method

The easiest way to get water out of a washing machine is often through the drain hose. This method uses gravity to remove most of the standing water. It's a simple, effective first step for many drainage issues. I find this approach works well for both top-load and front-load machines when the pump isn't running.

First, ensure you have completed the safety steps. This means the machine is unplugged, and the water supply is off. Gather your buckets and towels. Position the buckets on the floor near the washing machine. You will need them to collect the water.

Locate the drain hose at the back of the washing machine. It's a large, ribbed hose typically extending from the lower rear of the machine. Carefully detach the drain hose from its standpipe or laundry sink connection. Be ready for water to come out immediately. Hold the end of the hose firmly.

Lower the end of the drain hose into one of your empty buckets. Gravity will begin to pull the water from the drum. You may need to tilt the washing machine slightly to encourage more water to flow. This can be challenging alone. Have someone help you if the machine is heavy. As one bucket fills, quickly switch to an empty one. Continue this process until no more water drains from the hose. This method effectively removes the bulk of the water. For specific guidance on how to drain water from a Maytag washing machine, you can check [how to drain water from Maytag washing machine](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-drain-water-from-maytag-washing-machine).

### Step-by-Step Gravity Drainage

1.  **Prepare the Area:** Lay down old towels and position several large buckets on the floor. Make sure they are close to the machine. You will need them for collecting water.
2.  **Locate the Drain Hose:** Find the thick, corrugated drain hose at the rear of your washing machine. It usually connects to a standpipe or hooks over a laundry sink.
3.  **Disconnect the Hose:** Carefully unclip or pull the drain hose free from its connection. Be prepared for water to rush out. Keep the end of the hose elevated above the drum level for now.
4.  **Lower the Hose into a Bucket:** Hold the end of the drain hose firmly. Guide it into your first empty bucket. Lower the hose below the water level in the washing machine drum. Water will begin to flow out by gravity.
5.  **Collect the Water:** Allow the water to fill the bucket. As it gets full, pinch or temporarily lift the hose to stop the flow. Quickly switch to another empty bucket. Repeat this until the water flow slows or stops.
6.  **Clean Up:** Once most of the water is removed, use towels to soak up any remaining spills. Reattach the drain hose securely to its original position. ## Bailing Water Out: When Manual Drain Fails

Sometimes the drain hose method isn't enough, or it's not feasible. This happens if the hose itself is blocked or if you have a top-load machine with easy drum access. In these cases, you might need to manually bail water out of the drum. This process requires patience and effort. I have done this many times, and it is a good workout.

First, ensure all safety precautions are in place. The machine must be unplugged and water supply turned off. Gather plenty of buckets, old towels, and a smaller container for scooping. A small bowl, pitcher, or even a plastic cup works well. A wet-dry vacuum is also an excellent tool for this task if you own one.

Open the washing machine lid or door. If it is a front-load machine, you may need to prop the door open. Begin scooping water from the drum into your buckets. Work slowly and carefully to avoid splashing water everywhere. Fill your scoop, then pour the water into a bucket. This part can take some time, especially if the drum is full.

As the water level gets lower, it becomes harder to scoop. Use sponges or rags to soak up the last few inches of water. Wring them out into a bucket. Continue until the drum is as dry as possible. This method ensures you remove all standing water, preparing the machine for further inspection or repair. For example, if you own a Whirlpool washing machine, you can find specific guidance on [how to drain water in Whirlpool washing machine](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-drain-water-in-whirlpool-washing-machine). This will help with brand-specific procedures.

### Methods for Manual Water Removal

*   **Scooping with a Container:**
    *   Find a small, sturdy container like a pitcher, bowl, or large cup.
    *   Carefully dip the container into the drum. Scoop out as much water as possible.
    *   Pour the collected water into a large bucket.
    *   Repeat this process until the water level is too low to scoop effectively. This requires patience, but it is very effective.
*   **Using a Wet-Dry Vacuum:**
    *   A wet-dry vacuum (shop vac) is ideal for this task. It quickly sucks up large volumes of water.
    *   Insert the vacuum hose into the washing machine drum.
    *   Turn on the vacuum and let it suction the water.
    *   Empty the vacuum's tank as it fills. This saves a lot of manual effort and time. It is my preferred method for large amounts of water.
*   **Sponges and Towels for Residual Water:**
    *   Once scooping or vacuuming is complete, there will still be some water at the bottom.
    *   Use old sponges or absorbent towels to soak up this remaining water.
    *   Wring the sponges or towels into a bucket.
    *   Continue until the drum is completely dry. This prevents mold and mildew.
    *   Wipe down the interior of the drum to remove any remaining moisture.

## Checking and Clearing the Drain Pump Filter

After removing the bulk of the water, your next step is usually to check the drain pump filter. This is a very common cause of drainage problems. The filter is designed to catch lint, buttons, and other debris before they reach the pump. I always check this part first. A clogged filter prevents water from draining properly.

Most front-load washing machines have an accessible drain pump filter. It is typically located behind a small access panel at the bottom front of the machine. Some top-load machines also have them, but they might be harder to reach. Consult your owner's manual if you cannot find it. The manual shows the exact location and access method.

Once you locate the access panel, open it. You will see a cap or knob that belongs to the filter. Before unscrewing it, place a shallow pan or old towels directly underneath. There will always be some residual water in the pump housing. Slowly unscrew the filter cap. Water will start to trickle out. Let it drain into your pan or towels.

Once all the water has drained, remove the filter completely. Inspect it for any obstructions. You will likely find lint, hair, coins, or small items wrapped around it. Clean the filter thoroughly under running water. Use an old toothbrush if needed to scrub away grime. Also, check the filter housing for any debris that might have been left behind. Reinsert the clean filter and screw the cap back on tightly. A clean filter allows your machine to drain effectively.

### Step-by-Step Guide to Cleaning the Drain Pump Filter

1.  **Locate the Access Panel:** The drain pump filter is usually at the bottom front of your washing machine. Look for a small square or rectangular panel. It might snap open or require a screwdriver. Your owner's manual will show the exact spot.
2.  **Prepare for Water Spill:** Place a shallow pan or several layers of old towels directly beneath the filter access point. This catches any water that will drain out. Expect about a cup or two of water.
3.  **Unscrew the Filter Cap:** Gently unscrew the filter cap or knob. Do this slowly. Water will begin to drain from the pump housing into your prepared pan. Allow all the water to drain out.
4.  **Remove and Inspect the Filter:** Once the water stops, pull the filter all the way out. Examine it closely. You will likely find a collection of lint, hair, coins, buttons, or other small items. These are common culprits for drainage issues.
5.  **Clean the Filter:** Rinse the filter under warm, running water. Use your fingers or an old toothbrush to remove all debris. Make sure the filter screen is completely clear. Also, check inside the filter housing for any lingering objects.
6.  **Reinstall the Filter:** Once clean, carefully screw the filter back into its housing. Ensure it is tightened securely to prevent leaks. Close the access panel. Now, you can test if your washing machine drains properly. This simple cleaning often resolves the issue.

## Inspecting and Clearing the Drain Hose

If the drain pump filter is clean and your machine still won't drain, the drain hose is the next place to investigate. A kink or blockage in the hose will prevent water from exiting the machine. This is a common and often simple fix. I always look at the hose after the filter.

First, make sure the washing machine is unplugged and the water supply is off. Pull the washing machine away from the wall to access the back of the unit. This gives you full visibility of the drain hose. The hose connects to the back of the machine and typically extends to a standpipe or laundry sink.

Examine the entire length of the drain hose. Look for any sharp bends, kinks, or twists. These can severely restrict water flow. Straighten any kinks you find. The hose should run as straight as possible. If the hose is old and stiff, it might be prone to kinking. Consider replacing it if it keeps kinking.

Even without kinks, the hose can become blocked internally. Lint, soap scum, and other debris can build up over time. Disconnect the drain hose from both the washing machine and the standpipe/sink. Take it outside or to a utility sink. Flush water through it to check for obstructions. If water doesn't flow freely, you can try snaking it with a drain snake or using a strong blast of water. If the hose is visibly damaged or severely blocked, replacing it might be the easiest solution. A clear hose is vital for proper drainage.

### How to Check and Clear Your Washing Machine's Drain Hose

1.  **Ensure Safety:** Unplug the washing machine and turn off the hot and cold water supply valves.
2.  **Access the Hose:** Gently pull the washing machine away from the wall. This provides clear access to the rear of the machine. The drain hose is usually visible, extending from the bottom rear.
3.  **Inspect for Kinks:** Carefully examine the entire length of the drain hose. Look for any obvious bends, kinks, or sharp turns. These prevent water flow. Straighten any kinks you find. Ensure the hose has a gentle slope downwards if possible.
4.  **Disconnect the Hose:** Disconnect the drain hose from the standpipe or laundry sink. You might need pliers for a clamp. Then, disconnect it from the back of the washing machine. Be prepared for some residual water to spill out.
5.  **Check for Internal Blockages:** Take the disconnected hose to a utility sink or outside. Run water through it from a faucet. Watch the other end. If water flows slowly or not at all, you have an internal blockage.
6.  **Clear the Blockage:** If blocked, try to snake the hose with a plumbing snake. You can also try using a strong stream of water to dislodge debris. For stubborn blockages, a mixture of hot water and baking soda can sometimes help. If the hose is old, stiff, or severely damaged, replacing it is often the best option.
7.  **Reconnect and Test:** Once clear, securely reattach the drain hose to both the washing machine and the standpipe/sink. Make sure all connections are tight. Push the machine back into place, avoiding new kinks. Now, test a drain cycle to see if the issue is resolved. ## Dealing with a Clogged Standpipe or House Drain

Sometimes, the problem isn't with the washing machine itself, but with your home's plumbing. The washing machine drains into a standpipe or directly into a utility sink. If this part of your household plumbing becomes clogged, water from the washer has nowhere to go. It then backs up into the machine. I have seen this cause many mysterious drainage issues.

You can often identify a standpipe clog by observing the drain cycle. If water successfully exits the washing machine's drain hose but then overflows from the standpipe, the clog is in your house plumbing. Another sign is when other drains in your house, like a nearby sink, also drain slowly. This suggests a broader plumbing issue.

To address a clogged standpipe, first, ensure the washing machine's drain hose is removed from it. You can try a commercial drain cleaner designed for tough clogs. Follow the product's instructions carefully. Pour it down the standpipe and allow it to work for the recommended time. Afterward, flush the standpipe with hot water.

For more stubborn clogs, a plumbing snake might be necessary. Insert the snake into the standpipe and push it down until you hit the obstruction. Twist and push the snake to break up or retrieve the clog. If the clog is deep or you are uncomfortable using a snake, it's best to call a professional plumber. A clear standpipe ensures proper drainage for your washing machine.

### Clearing Plumbing Blockages

1.  **Identify the Clog Location:** Observe the water flow. If water exits the washing machine's drain hose but backs up or overflows from the standpipe, the clog is in your home's plumbing system, not the washer itself. This means the washing machine parts are likely fine.
2.  **Remove Washer Hose:** Take the washing machine's drain hose out of the standpipe. Place it into an empty bucket if the machine still holds water. This prevents more water from adding to the clog.
3.  **Try a Commercial Drain Cleaner:** Use a chemical drain cleaner. These solutions are formulated to dissolve common blockages like hair and grease. Pour the recommended amount into the standpipe. Wait for the specified time, then flush with hot water. Always read and follow the manufacturer's safety instructions.
4.  **Use a Plumbing Snake:** If the drain cleaner doesn't work, a plumbing snake is often effective. Insert the snake into the standpipe. Push it down until you feel resistance. Rotate and push the snake to break apart the clog. Pull the snake back to retrieve any debris.
5.  **Check Other Drains:** If multiple drains in your home are slow, the clog might be deeper in your main plumbing line. In such cases, attempting DIY fixes could worsen the problem.
6.  **When to Call a Plumber:** If the clog persists after these attempts, or if you suspect a major plumbing issue, contact a professional plumber. They have specialized tools and expertise to clear deep or stubborn blockages safely. Knowing when to get professional help saves you time and prevents further damage. ## Troubleshooting a Faulty Drain Pump

If you have cleared the drain hose, cleaned the filter, and confirmed no external plumbing clogs, the drain pump itself might be faulty. The drain pump is a crucial component that actively forces water out of the washing machine. When it fails, water simply sits in the drum. This is a common part to wear out over time.

You can often diagnose a faulty drain pump by listening to the machine during a drain cycle. If you hear a humming or buzzing sound but no water is moving, the pump motor might be running, but the impeller is jammed or broken. If you hear nothing at all, the motor itself might be bad. A completely silent machine during the drain cycle is also a strong indicator.

Accessing the drain pump varies by machine model. For many front-load washers, it's near the drain filter behind the bottom access panel. For top-load washers, you might need to tilt the machine back or remove the front or back panel. Always consult your owner's manual for specific access instructions. Remember to unplug the machine and turn off water before beginning.

Once you access the pump, check for any physical obstructions first. Sometimes, a foreign object like a sock or a piece of plastic can get past the filter and jam the impeller. Remove any visible debris. If the impeller spins freely and there's no obstruction, the pump motor itself is likely defective. Replacing a drain pump is a more advanced DIY repair. It involves disconnecting wires and hoses. If you are not comfortable with this, consider calling a professional appliance technician.

### Signs and Solutions for a Broken Drain Pump

1.  **Symptoms of a Faulty Pump:**
    *   **Humming or Buzzing Sound:** The machine tries to drain, but water stays in the drum. This means the motor runs, but the impeller cannot spin. It may be jammed by debris.
    *   **No Sound During Drain Cycle:** The machine remains silent when it should be draining. This indicates a complete motor failure or electrical issue with the pump.
    *   **Water Remains After Spin:** Even after the spin cycle, the drum remains full of water. This shows the pump did not activate or failed to clear the water.
2.  **Accessing the Drain Pump:**
    *   **Safety First:** Unplug the washing machine and turn off the water supply completely.
    *   **Locate the Pump:** Refer to your owner's manual. The drain pump is typically at the bottom of the machine. It might be behind a front access panel or require tilting the machine back to access from the bottom.
    *   **Prepare for Water:** Have towels and a pan ready. There will be residual water in the pump and hoses.
3.  **Inspecting for Obstructions:**
    *   Once you can access the pump, look inside the pump housing. Small items can bypass the filter and jam the impeller.
    *   Use pliers or your fingers to carefully remove any debris you find. Ensure the impeller can spin freely.
4.  **Testing the Pump (Advanced):**
    *   If no obstructions are found, the pump motor might be bad. You can use a multimeter to test for continuity or voltage at the pump. This requires electrical knowledge.
    *   **Warning:** Do not attempt electrical testing if you are unsure. This can be dangerous.
5.  **Replacing the Drain Pump:**
    *   If the pump is confirmed faulty, it needs replacement. This involves disconnecting the pump from its hoses and electrical wiring.
    *   Order a new pump specific to your washing machine model.
    *   Carefully install the new pump, ensuring all connections are secure.
    *   This is a moderately complex repair. Many people prefer to hire a professional technician for this task.
