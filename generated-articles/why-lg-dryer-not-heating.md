## Why Your LG Dryer Isn't Heating: Quick Fixes & Expert Tips

Imagine a pile of damp laundry, still cold after a full dryer cycle. This is a common and frustrating problem: your LG dryer is not heating. I have faced this issue myself, and it is never a good feeling. A dryer that runs but produces no heat leaves clothes wet and adds to your chores.

Understanding why your LG dryer stops heating is the first step to getting your laundry routine back on track. We will explore the most common reasons behind this problem. From simple clogs to more complex part failures, this guide covers everything. You will learn actionable steps to diagnose and potentially fix your LG dryer.

### Takeaway

*   **Check the Basics First:** Ensure the dryer has power and that the circuit breaker is not tripped.
*   **Clean Lint Filter and Vent:** These are the most common causes of heating problems. A clogged vent can cause the dryer to overheat and trip safety devices.
*   **Inspect Thermal Fuse and Thermostats:** These safety components cut power to the heating element if temperatures get too high. They often require replacement if blown.
*   **Test the Heating Element (Electric) or Igniter/Gas Valve (Gas):** These are core components responsible for producing heat.
*   **Consider Professional Help:** For complex electrical issues or if you are unsure, contact a certified technician.

**Why is my LG dryer not heating?**

Your LG dryer is not heating often due to a clogged lint filter or exhaust vent, a blown thermal fuse, a faulty heating element, or a tripped circuit breaker. Less common causes include a bad thermistor, cycling thermostat, or a control board issue.

### Understanding Your LG Dryer's Heating System

It helps to know how your LG dryer creates heat. An electric dryer uses a heating element, which is a coiled wire that generates heat when electricity flows through it. A gas dryer uses an igniter to light natural gas or propane, creating a flame to heat the air. Both types use a fan to draw in air, heat it, and then circulate it through the drum to dry your clothes.

Sensors and thermostats regulate the temperature. These components ensure the dryer operates safely and efficiently. If any part of this system fails, your LG dryer will stop heating. We need to identify which part is causing the issue.

I have found that understanding the basics makes troubleshooting much easier. Knowing what each part does helps pinpoint the problem faster. This knowledge saves time and potential repair costs.

### Common Culprits: Clogged Vents & Lint Filters

One of the most frequent reasons an LG dryer stops heating is poor airflow. This might sound simple, but it is incredibly common. When air cannot flow freely, the dryer overheats. Safety mechanisms then shut off the heat to prevent a fire.

Your dryer's lint filter is the first line of defense. It catches lint from your clothes. If this filter is full, air cannot pass through easily. This causes the dryer to work harder and get hotter.

*   **Lint Filter:** Always clean the lint filter before or after every load. This takes seconds and can prevent major issues. I make it a habit to clean mine every time I use the dryer.
*   **Exhaust Vent:** The exhaust vent hose runs from the back of your dryer to an outside vent. Over time, lint builds up in this hose. This blockage is a major fire hazard and will cause your dryer to stop heating. You should clean your dryer vent at least once a year, possibly more often if you use your dryer frequently or have pets. A special brush or a vacuum cleaner can help clear this lint. This simple cleaning step often solves the problem of an LG dryer not heating. To understand how proper cleaning contributes to your dryer's performance, you can read more on [how to clean LG dryer](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-clean-lg-dryer).

Remember, restricted airflow is a primary cause for many dryer heating problems. Addressing this first can save you from deeper diagnostics. It is often the simplest fix.

### Faulty Heating Elements: A Major Cause for No Heat

If your electric LG dryer is not heating, a faulty heating element is a likely suspect. The heating element is a metal coil that glows red hot when working correctly. It is the primary component responsible for generating heat. Over time, these coils can break or burn out due to constant heating and cooling cycles.

When the element breaks, electricity cannot flow through it. This means no heat is produced inside the dryer drum. You might still hear the dryer running, tumbling clothes, but they will stay cold. I have seen this issue many times, and it is a clear sign that the element needs checking.

*   **How to Check:** You will need a multimeter to test the heating element for continuity. First, unplug your dryer for safety. Then, access the heating element, usually located behind a panel on the back or bottom of the dryer. Disconnect the wires and place the multimeter probes on the element's terminals. A good element will show continuity (a low ohm reading). No continuity means the element is broken and needs replacement. Replacing a heating element can seem daunting, but it is a common repair. You might find helpful information on how to handle this by looking into [how to reset LG dryer heating element](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-reset-lg-dryer-heating-element), which often involves checking the element itself.

Replacing a heating element is a repair many homeowners can do. Always buy a new element specifically designed for your LG dryer model. If your LG dryer is running but not heating, and it's an electric model, the heating element is often the problem. You might find more details on this specific symptom at [why is my LG dryer running but not heating](https://homeessentialsguide.com/why-is-my-lg-dryer-running-but-not-heating).

### Blown Thermal Fuses & Thermostats: Safety First

LG dryers include several safety devices to prevent overheating. The thermal fuse and cycling thermostat are two such critical components. They monitor the temperature inside the dryer. If the temperature gets too high, they cut off power to the heating element.

A thermal fuse is a one-time safety device. If it blows, it must be replaced. It is typically located near the blower housing or heating element. A blown thermal fuse often indicates a deeper issue, like a clogged vent, causing the dryer to overheat. Always check for airflow blockages if you find a blown thermal fuse.

The cycling thermostat, on the other hand, cycles the heat on and off to maintain a consistent temperature. If it fails, it can either fail open (no heat) or fail closed (overheating). Both are problematic for your drying process. I often test these components if the heating element tests fine.

*   **Testing:** You can test both the thermal fuse and cycling thermostat with a multimeter for continuity. Unplug the dryer first. Remove the wires from the component you are testing. Place the multimeter probes on the terminals. A good component will show continuity. If the thermal fuse shows no continuity, it is blown and needs replacement. If the cycling thermostat shows no continuity, it is likely faulty. This is a common reason why an [LG dryer is not heating up](https://homeessentialsguide.com/why-isnt-my-lg-dryer-heating-up).

Replacing these parts is usually straightforward once you gain access to them. Always ensure you are replacing them with parts specific to your LG dryer model. These safety components are vital; never bypass them.

### Troubleshooting Gas Dryers: Igniters and Gas Valves

If you have a gas LG dryer that is not heating, the issues are different from electric models. Gas dryers rely on a gas valve and an igniter to produce heat. The igniter heats up to light the gas, creating a flame. If either of these components fails, your dryer will not generate heat.

You might notice the dryer tumbling clothes but no warm air, or perhaps a faint gas smell without ignition. This suggests an issue with the gas delivery or ignition system. Safety is paramount when dealing with gas appliances. I always recommend caution and, if unsure, calling a professional.

*   **Igniter:** The igniter is a glow bar that gets extremely hot to ignite the gas. If it is cracked, broken, or not glowing, it will not ignite the gas. You can visually inspect the igniter (unplug the dryer first). If it is glowing but not igniting gas, the gas valve might be the problem. You can also test the igniter for continuity with a multimeter, although a visual check often reveals the issue.
*   **Gas Valve Solenoids:** The gas valve has solenoids that open to release gas when the igniter is hot enough. If these solenoids fail, gas will not flow to the burner. You can test these solenoids for continuity with a multimeter. Often, if the igniter glows but no flame appears, it points to faulty gas valve solenoids.

Always ensure the gas supply to your dryer is turned on. Also, check that other gas appliances in your home are working. If not, the issue might be with your home's gas supply. If you suspect a gas leak, evacuate and contact your gas provider immediately. If you have an [electric dryer and it is not heating](https://homeessentialsguide.com/why-electric-dryer-is-not-heating), then these specific gas components are not relevant to your problem.

### Defective Thermistors or Moisture Sensors

Modern LG dryers use advanced sensors to control drying cycles. The thermistor and moisture sensors play crucial roles in this. A thermistor measures the temperature inside the dryer. It sends data to the control board, which then adjusts the heating element's operation. If the thermistor gives inaccurate readings or fails, the dryer might not heat at all or might overheat.

Moisture sensors detect the dampness of your clothes. They tell the dryer when the clothes are dry, allowing it to shut off the cycle. If these sensors are dirty or faulty, the dryer might think clothes are dry even when they are still wet. This can cause the dryer to shorten cycles and not provide enough heat time. I have seen cases where dirty sensors lead to seemingly under-dried clothes.

*   **Cleaning Moisture Sensors:** The moisture sensors are typically two metal strips located inside the dryer drum, near the lint filter. Over time, fabric softener residue or lint can coat these sensors. This prevents them from accurately detecting moisture. You can clean them gently with a cotton swab and rubbing alcohol. This simple step often restores proper drying.
*   **Testing Thermistor:** A thermistor changes its resistance based on temperature. You can test a thermistor with a multimeter to measure its resistance at room temperature. Then, compare the reading to your dryer's service manual specifications. If the readings are way off, the thermistor is likely faulty and needs replacement. While you might want to try to [reset your LG electric dryer if it's not heating](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-reset-lg-electric-dryer-not-heating), checking these sensors can also be a key diagnostic step.

If these sensors are malfunctioning, the dryer's brain (the control board) gets bad information. This leads to incorrect heating or drying times. Always ensure these sensors are clean and functioning correctly for optimal dryer performance.

### Control Board Issues: The Brain of Your Dryer

The control board is essentially the "brain" of your LG dryer. It receives signals from all the sensors and switches. Then, it sends commands to the heating element, motor, and other components. If the control board malfunctions, it can prevent the dryer from heating. This can happen even if all other components are in working order.

A faulty control board can manifest in several ways. The dryer might not start, buttons might not respond, or it might just not heat. Sometimes, you might see error codes on the display. These codes often point to specific component failures, which might indirectly indicate a board issue if the component itself tests fine.

*   **Diagnosing a Control Board:** Diagnosing a bad control board can be tricky. It is usually the last component to suspect after ruling out all other common issues. You cannot easily test a control board with a multimeter like other parts. Instead, diagnosis often involves a process of elimination. If all other components (heating element, fuses, thermostats, igniter, sensors) test good, then the control board is the likely culprit.

*   **Replacement:** Replacing a control board is typically expensive. It often requires careful handling and precise installation. Due to the cost and complexity, it is often a job best left to a qualified appliance technician. Before considering a control board replacement, double-check all simpler fixes. Ensure the power supply is stable. Sometimes, a power surge can damage the control board. If your [LG dryer is not heating up](https://homeessentialsguide.com/why-isnt-my-lg-dryer-heating-up) and you have checked everything else, the control board might be the problem.

A control board failure is less common than issues like clogged vents or bad heating elements. However, it is a possibility for persistent no-heat problems.

### Power Supply Problems: Ensuring Adequate Power

Sometimes, your LG dryer isn't heating because it is not getting proper power. This is less about a faulty dryer part and more about your home's electrical system. Electric dryers require a 240-volt circuit. They use two separate circuit breakers in your electrical panel. If one of these breakers trips, the dryer will still get 120 volts. This means the motor might run, the drum might spin, and the control panel might light up, but the heating element will not receive enough power to generate heat.

It is easy to overlook this simple check. I always advise people to look at their circuit breaker panel first. A tripped breaker might look slightly off or be fully flipped to the "off" position. Flipping it completely off then back on can reset it.

*   **Checking the Breaker:** Go to your home's main electrical panel. Locate the circuit breakers labeled for your dryer. They are usually two linked breakers. If one or both are tripped (often halfway between ON and OFF), firmly flip them all the way to OFF, then back to ON.
*   **Outlet Check:** Make sure the dryer is fully plugged into its dedicated wall outlet. Sometimes, a loose plug can cause intermittent power issues.
*   **Voltage Test (Advanced):** If you are comfortable using a multimeter, you can test the dryer outlet for proper voltage. An electric dryer outlet should show around 240 volts across the two hot slots. If it shows only 120 volts, you have a tripped breaker or a wiring issue in your home's electrical system. This is an electrical problem that likely requires an electrician, not an appliance repair person. This is often the fundamental cause when your LG dryer stops heating.

Always ensure the dryer's power cord is not damaged. A frayed or burnt cord can also cause power issues and is a fire hazard. Address any power supply issues before troubleshooting dryer components.

### Professional Help vs. DIY: When to Call a Technician

Many LG dryer heating issues can be fixed with basic tools and some patience. Cleaning lint traps, unclogging vents, and even replacing thermal fuses or heating elements are common DIY repairs. I often tackle these kinds of fixes myself. However, there are times when calling a professional technician is the smarter choice.

Knowing when to seek expert help can save you time, frustration, and even potential safety risks. Your safety is always the priority. Working with gas lines or complex electrical components without proper training can be dangerous.

*   **When to DIY:**
    *   **Simple Maintenance:** Cleaning lint filters and exhaust vents.
    *   **Basic Part Replacement:** Thermal fuses, thermostats, and heating elements (for electric dryers) are often straightforward. There are many online guides, and parts are readily available.
    *   **Clear Diagnosis:** If you have definitively identified the faulty part through testing.

*   **When to Call a Professional:**
    *   **Gas Dryer Issues:** Any problem involving the gas valve or gas lines should be handled by a certified gas appliance technician. Gas leaks are extremely dangerous.
    *   **Electrical Wiring:** If you suspect issues with the dryer's main power supply, the wall outlet, or internal wiring that looks burnt or frayed.
    *   **Control Board Problems:** As discussed, diagnosing and replacing a control board is complex and costly.
    *   **No Clear Diagnosis:** If you have checked all the common culprits and tested components but cannot find the problem.
    *   **Lack of Confidence:** If you are uncomfortable working with electricity or gas, or simply do not feel confident in your repair skills.
    *   **Warranty:** If your LG dryer is still under warranty, attempting a DIY repair might void it. Check your warranty terms first.

A professional can quickly diagnose complex issues and perform repairs safely and efficiently. ## FAQ Section

### Why is my LG dryer running but not heating?
If your LG dryer runs but does not heat, the issue is likely a failed heating component. This often points to a blown thermal fuse, a broken heating element (for electric dryers), or faulty gas valve solenoids/igniter (for gas dryers). A clogged exhaust vent can also cause safety devices to cut power to the heat.

### Can I reset my LG dryer if it's not heating?
You can try resetting your LG dryer. First, unplug the dryer from the wall for at least 5-10 minutes. Then, check your home's circuit breaker panel for a tripped breaker and reset it if needed. Plug the dryer back in. This can sometimes clear minor electronic glitches.

### How much does it cost to fix an LG dryer heating element?
The cost to fix an LG dryer heating element varies. The part itself typically costs between $50 and $200. If you perform the repair yourself, that is your only cost. If you hire a professional, labor charges can add another $100-$300, bringing the total to $150-$500 depending on your location and the technician's rates.

### How do I know if my thermal fuse is bad on my LG dryer?
You can test an LG dryer's thermal fuse with a multimeter. First, unplug the dryer. Disconnect the wires from the fuse. Set your multimeter to the continuity setting. Place the probes on each terminal of the fuse. If the multimeter does not show continuity (or beeps), the thermal fuse is bad and needs replacement.

### What causes an LG dryer heating element to go bad?
An LG dryer heating element typically goes bad due to prolonged use, eventually burning out. Poor airflow from clogged lint filters or exhaust vents is a common culprit. Restricted airflow causes the element to overheat and wear out faster. Power surges or age can also contribute to its failure.

### Is it worth repairing an old LG dryer that isn't heating?
Deciding whether to repair an old LG dryer depends on its age, the cost of the repair, and the dryer's overall condition. If the repair is simple and inexpensive (like a thermal fuse or a heating element for a relatively young dryer), it is often worth it. For very old dryers or expensive repairs (like a control board), buying a new, more energy-efficient model might be a better long-term investment.
