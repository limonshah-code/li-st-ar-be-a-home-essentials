## Fixing Your Dishwasher When It Won't Drain

Imagine loading your dishwasher, running a cycle, and returning to find standing, murky water at the bottom. It feels frustrating, right? A dishwasher that won't drain means dirty dishes stay dirty, and your kitchen routine hits a snag. This common appliance problem often signals a simple blockage or a minor part malfunction.

You might feel overwhelmed, thinking about expensive service calls. But many drainage issues have straightforward fixes you can do yourself. This guide will show you exactly how to fix a dishwasher that won't drain. We will explore the common reasons behind this problem. We will give you clear, step-by-step troubleshooting instructions. By the end, you will have the knowledge to get your dishwasher working properly again, saving you time and money.

### Takeaway

*   Regularly clean the dishwasher filter to prevent food particle buildup.
*   Inspect the drain hose for kinks or clogs, ensuring a clear path for water.
*   Check the garbage disposal and air gap for obstructions if your dishwasher connects to them.
*   Diagnose the drain pump for foreign objects or signs of mechanical failure.
*   Perform a power reset to clear any electronic glitches affecting drainage.

### Clear, Concise Answer

A dishwasher that won't drain often has a clogged filter, a blocked drain hose, a malfunctioning drain pump, or an issue with the air gap or garbage disposal connection. Fixing it involves systematically checking these components for obstructions or failures, clearing any blockages, or replacing faulty parts.

## Understand Why Your Dishwasher Won't Drain

It is never fun to open your dishwasher and see a pool of dirty water. This common issue happens for several reasons. Understanding these reasons helps you fix the problem faster. Most times, the cause is a simple obstruction or a small part failure.

Food particles often collect in the filter at the bottom of the dishwasher. This buildup creates a barrier, stopping water from flowing out. Another frequent culprit is the drain hose. This hose carries water away from the appliance. It can get kinked, twisted, or clogged with debris. Sometimes, a foreign object falls into the dishwasher and blocks the drain pump's impeller. The drain pump moves water out of the dishwasher. If it breaks, water stays inside.

External connections also play a role. If your dishwasher drains into a garbage disposal, the disposal needs to be clear. A clogged disposal prevents proper drainage. Similarly, a clogged air gap on your countertop can stop water flow. The air gap prevents dirty water from flowing back into your clean water supply. The check valve also prevents backflow. If it is stuck, water will not drain. Knowing these common issues prepares you for successful troubleshooting. This helps you get your dishwasher back to normal function. ## Essential First Checks: Filters, Hoses, and Water Levels

When your dishwasher refuses to drain, starting with the most common and easiest fixes makes sense. These initial checks do not require special tools or deep technical knowledge. They address the issues that most frequently cause drainage problems. Taking these steps can quickly solve your problem and prevent further frustration.

### Clearing the Dishwasher Filter

The dishwasher filter is your first stop. It sits at the bottom of the dishwasher tub. Its job is to catch food particles and debris, protecting the drain pump. Over time, these particles accumulate, forming a thick sludge that blocks water flow. If water cannot pass through the filter, it cannot reach the drain pump, and thus, it cannot drain.

To clean the filter, first, remove the lower dish rack. You will see a cylindrical or flat filter assembly. Turn it counter-clockwise or unclip it, depending on your model. Lift it out carefully. Take the filter to your sink. Rinse it under warm, running water. Use a soft brush, like an old toothbrush, to scrub away any stuck-on food or grease. Dish soap can help cut through grime. Ensure all mesh parts are clear. Once clean, place it back into the dishwasher. Twist or clip it firmly into place. This simple step often solves the "dishwasher won't drain" problem. Regular cleaning prevents future clogs. You can find more information about its location here: [where is the drain in my dishwasher](https://homeessentialsguide.com/where-is-the-drain-in-my-dishwasher).

### Inspecting the Dishwasher Drain Hose

The drain hose is crucial for removing water from your dishwasher. This hose usually runs from the bottom of the dishwasher, under the sink, and connects to either your garbage disposal or a dedicated drain pipe. It is susceptible to kinks and clogs. Kinks restrict water flow. Clogs, often from food debris or grease, can completely block the hose.

First, pull the dishwasher out slightly if needed to access the hose behind it. Look for any visible bends or twists in the hose. Straighten any kinks you find. Next, check the hose connection points, especially under the sink. You might see a clamp. If you suspect a clog, you may need to disconnect the hose. Place a bucket underneath to catch any water. Use a wet/dry vacuum to suction out any debris. Alternatively, you can use a plumber's snake to dislodge stubborn clogs. Reconnect the hose securely once clear.

### Confirming Proper Water Levels and Float Switch Function

Your dishwasher uses a float switch to detect water levels. This switch prevents the dishwasher from overfilling. It also tells the control board when water has reached a certain level. If the float switch gets stuck in the "full" position, the dishwasher may not start a new cycle. More importantly, it might think there is already too much water and refuse to drain the existing water.

Locate the float switch, typically a small, plastic dome or cylinder at the bottom of the dishwasher tub. Push it up and down. It should move freely without resistance. Sometimes, food particles or detergent buildup can stick the float in place. Clean around the float with a damp cloth. Make sure nothing obstructs its movement. If the float itself appears damaged or does not spring back, it might need replacement. A properly functioning float ensures the dishwasher correctly manages water levels, allowing for proper draining.

## Addressing External Drainage System Issues

Sometimes, the problem lies outside the dishwasher itself. The appliance relies on other parts of your kitchen's plumbing system to function correctly. Issues with your garbage disposal, air gap, or even the main kitchen sink drain can directly prevent your dishwasher from draining. These external components are just as important to check.

### Verifying Your Garbage Disposal is Clear

Many dishwashers connect directly to the kitchen garbage disposal unit. If you have a disposal, it is a common point of failure for dishwasher drainage. The disposal has a dedicated inlet for the dishwasher drain hose. If the disposal is full of food waste, or if it is jammed, water cannot flow from the dishwasher.

First, run your garbage disposal with cold water for at least a minute. Listen for any unusual noises that might indicate a jam. If it sounds like it is straining, use the reset button on the bottom of the disposal or manually turn the flywheel with an Allen wrench. After clearing any jam, run it again. This ensures a clear path for water. If you recently installed a new garbage disposal, you must also check for a knockout plug. Manufacturers often leave a plug in the dishwasher inlet. You need to remove this plug for your dishwasher to drain. Use a screwdriver and hammer to punch it out.

### Cleaning the Dishwasher Air Gap

An air gap is a small, chrome cylinder on your kitchen countertop, usually next to the faucet. It serves an important safety purpose. It prevents dirty dishwasher water from siphoning back into your clean drinking water supply. When an air gap gets clogged with food particles or mineral deposits, it restricts the dishwasher's ability to drain water. You might even see water squirting from the air gap cover.

To clean it, twist off the top cover of the air gap. Then, remove the inner cap. You will see two hoses connected to the bottom. Inspect the inside of the air gap for any debris. Use a toothpick or a small brush to clear out any gunk. You can also run water through the hoses to flush them. Reassemble the air gap, ensuring both caps are seated properly. A clean air gap ensures proper ventilation and flow for your dishwasher's drainage system.

### Assessing Your Kitchen Sink Drain

Your dishwasher shares a common drain line with your kitchen sink. If your sink drains slowly or is completely clogged, it will inevitably affect your dishwasher's ability to drain. A blockage further down the main drain pipe can cause water to back up, not just in your sink, but also in your dishwasher. This happens because the water has nowhere else to go.

To check this, fill your kitchen sink halfway with water. Pull the stopper and observe how quickly it drains. If it drains slowly or not at all, you have a sink drain clog. You can try a simple home remedy. Pour one cup of baking soda down the drain, followed by one cup of white vinegar. Let it sit for 15-30 minutes, then flush with hot water. This mixture helps break down grease and minor clogs. If the sink still drains poorly, the clog might be deeper in your plumbing system. In this case, you might need a plumber.

## Diagnosing and Fixing the Dishwasher Drain Pump

If external components are clear and your initial checks did not solve the problem, the drain pump might be the culprit. The drain pump is the heart of your dishwasher's drainage system. It uses a motor to force water out of the appliance and into the drain line. When this pump malfunctions, water cannot exit the tub, leading to standing water.

### Identifying Drain Pump Malfunction

You can often tell if the drain pump is failing by listening carefully. During the drain cycle, a working pump makes a distinct whirring sound as it pushes water out. If you hear a loud humming noise but no water movement, the pump's motor might be running, but its impeller is jammed. If you hear no sound at all during the drain cycle, the pump might not be getting power, or it has completely failed. Another sign is if water drains slowly or not at all, even after you have cleared filters and hoses.

Before you inspect the drain pump, you must cut power to the dishwasher. Locate your home's circuit breaker box and flip the breaker for the kitchen or dishwasher. Alternatively, unplug the appliance if possible. This prevents electric shock. Then, remove all standing water from the dishwasher tub using a cup and towels. This prepares the area for inspection.

### Accessing and Inspecting the Drain Pump

Accessing the drain pump varies by dishwasher model. For many dishwashers, you can reach the pump from inside the tub. You might need to remove the lower spray arm and then the filter assembly. After that, you could see a pump cover or impeller access plate. For other models, you might need to pull the dishwasher out from its cabinet and access the pump from underneath. This usually involves tilting the dishwasher on its side or back.

Once you gain access, carefully inspect the impeller. The impeller is a small fan-like component inside the pump. It spins to move water. Look for any foreign objects tangled in the impeller or blocking it. Common items include broken glass, food seeds, small bones, or plastic pieces. Use pliers or tweezers to remove any obstructions. Ensure the impeller spins freely by hand. If it is stuck, carefully try to free it. Sometimes, just clearing debris solves the problem. ### Testing and Replacing a Faulty Drain Pump

If the pump impeller is clear but the dishwasher still does not drain, the pump itself might be faulty. You can test the pump for electrical continuity using a multimeter. Set the multimeter to the ohms (Î©) setting. Disconnect the electrical wires from the pump motor. Touch the multimeter probes to the pump's terminals. A reading of infinity or no continuity suggests an open circuit, meaning the pump motor is dead. A reading of "0" or close to "0" indicates a short circuit. Either case points to a faulty pump.

Replacing a drain pump involves disconnecting the old one and installing a new one. This usually requires basic tools. You need to ensure you get the correct replacement part for your specific dishwasher model. Take a photo of the old pump and its connections before removal. Follow the manufacturer's instructions for removal and installation. After installation, reconnect all wires and secure the pump. Then, power up the dishwasher and run a test cycle.
