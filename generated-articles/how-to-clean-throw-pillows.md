## Refresh Your Home: How To Clean Throw Pillows Effectively

Throw pillows bring comfort and style to any living space. They add color, texture, and a cozy feel to sofas, chairs, and beds. However, these decorative items also attract dust, dirt, spills, and pet dander over time. A dirty throw pillow can detract from your home's appearance and affect indoor air quality. Learning **how to clean throw pillows** is essential for maintaining their beauty and extending their life.

Proper cleaning keeps your pillows looking fresh and inviting. It also helps remove allergens and odors that accumulate with daily use. This comprehensive guide will walk you through every step of the process. We cover identifying your pillow's fabric, choosing the right cleaning method, tackling stubborn stains, and routine maintenance. By the end, you will feel confident refreshing all your decorative pillows.

**Takeaway:**
*   Always check the care label before cleaning any throw pillow.
*   Separate removable pillow covers from their inserts when possible.
*   Address small stains promptly with spot cleaning techniques.
*   Machine wash covers on a gentle cycle if the care tag permits.
*   Hand wash delicate fabrics to prevent damage.
*   Ensure pillows and covers dry completely to avoid mildew.

To effectively clean throw pillows, always check the care tag first. Most have removable covers that can be machine or hand washed. For non-removable covers or delicate fabrics, spot clean or dry clean. Ensure thorough drying to prevent mildew and maintain shape.

### Understanding Your Throw Pillow: The First Step to Cleaning

Before you begin any cleaning process, you must understand your throw pillow. Each pillow is unique, made from different materials that require specific care. Ignoring these details can lead to damage, shrinking, or color fading. I always start by checking the small tag sewn into the pillow or cover. This care label is your most important resource.

The care label provides instructions for washing, drying, and ironing. It will tell you if the item is machine washable, hand wash only, or dry clean only. Sometimes, it specifies temperature settings or detergents to use. If a tag is missing, you must identify the fabric type yourself. Common materials include cotton, linen, silk, velvet, wool, and various synthetics. Each of these materials reacts differently to water and cleaning agents. For a general overview of whether your pillows can be washed, explore [can you wash throw pillows](https://homeessentialsguide.com/can-you-wash-throw-pillows).

The pillow insert material also matters. Down and feather inserts require different care than foam or fiberfill. Some inserts can be washed, while others cannot. Knowing both the cover and insert materials guides your cleaning choices. Never assume all pillows are the same; a little research prevents big mistakes. Following these initial steps ensures you choose the safest and most effective method for your specific throw pillow. For general tips on how to clean pillows, you can refer to [clean pillows how to](https://homeessentialsguide.com/clean-pillows-how-to).

### Preparing Your Throw Pillows for Cleaning

Proper preparation makes a big difference in the success of your cleaning efforts. Taking a few moments to prepare your throw pillows can prevent further issues and make the actual cleaning easier. I always start by inspecting each pillow carefully. Look for any loose threads, tears, or embellishments that might require special attention.

If your throw pillow has a removable cover, gently unzip it and remove the insert. Many decorative pillows have zippers hidden along a seam, making this step simple. Washing covers separately from inserts is often the best approach. This allows you to treat each component according to its specific care needs. Once the cover is off, give it a good shake outdoors to dislodge loose dust and crumbs. You can also use a soft-bristled brush or a vacuum cleaner with an upholstery attachment to remove surface dirt. This step prevents dirt from turning into mud during washing.

Next, you should test any cleaning solution you plan to use. Apply a small amount of the solution to an inconspicuous area of the fabric, such as the back corner or inside a seam. Wait a few minutes to see if any discoloration or damage occurs. This patch test is vital, especially for vibrant colors or delicate fabrics. Finally, pre-treat any visible stains before washing. Addressing stains directly improves their chances of removal. ### Machine Washing Throw Pillow Covers Safely

Machine washing is often the easiest and most convenient method for cleaning throw pillow covers. However, it is only suitable for covers clearly marked as machine washable on their care labels. Always double-check this information to avoid damaging your covers. Cotton, linen, and many synthetic covers are typically machine friendly.

When machine washing, always use cold water. Cold water helps prevent colors from bleeding and fabrics from shrinking. Select a gentle cycle on your washing machine. This setting mimics hand washing, reducing agitation that can harm delicate materials or embellishments. I recommend placing covers in a mesh laundry bag. This offers an extra layer of protection, especially for covers with zippers or delicate trims. Use a mild detergent, preferably one formulated for delicate items. Avoid harsh chemicals or bleach unless specifically stated on the care label for stain removal. Never overload your washing machine. Give the covers enough space to move freely during the wash cycle. Overloading can prevent thorough cleaning and cause damage.

#### Drying Machine-Washed Pillow Covers

Proper drying is just as important as washing. After the wash cycle, remove the pillow covers promptly. To prevent shrinking and preserve fabric integrity, air drying is generally the safest option. You can lay the covers flat on a clean towel or hang them on a clothesline or drying rack. Make sure to reshape them gently while damp. This helps them maintain their original form.

If you choose to use a dryer, select the lowest heat setting. High heat can cause significant shrinkage, especially in cotton and linen fabrics. I usually add a few clean, dry towels to the dryer. These help absorb moisture and fluff the covers. Check the covers frequently during the drying process. Remove them while they are still slightly damp to minimize wrinkles and prevent over-drying. Ensure they are completely dry before putting them back on the inserts. Dampness can lead to mildew and unpleasant odors. ### Hand Washing Delicate Throw Pillows and Covers

Sometimes, machine washing is not an option. Delicate fabrics like silk, velvet, or wool often require a more gentle approach. Throw pillows with non-removable covers, intricate embroidery, or beaded details also benefit from hand washing. This method allows you to control the cleaning process precisely, protecting fragile materials from damage. I find hand washing gives me peace of mind when dealing with cherished items.

To hand wash, start by filling a clean basin or tub with cool water. Add a small amount of mild liquid detergent. Choose a detergent specifically designed for delicate garments or a pH-neutral soap. Swirl the water gently to create a soapy solution. Submerge the pillow cover or the entire pillow if it is suitable for wet cleaning. Gently squeeze the item, allowing the soapy water to penetrate the fabric. Avoid harsh scrubbing or twisting, which can stretch or damage fibers. Focus on areas with visible stains, treating them with extra care. Let the item soak for about 10-15 minutes, depending on its soil level. After soaking, drain the soapy water. Refill the basin with clean, cool water and rinse the item thoroughly. Continue rinsing until all soap residue is gone.

#### Proper Drying for Hand-Washed Items

Drying hand-washed items correctly is critical to prevent damage. Do not wring out the pillow or cover aggressively. Instead, gently press excess water out of the fabric. You can roll the item in a clean, dry towel to absorb more moisture. This step removes much of the water without distorting the fabric. Once excess water is removed, reshape the pillow or cover to its original form.

Lay the item flat on a clean, dry surface or a mesh drying rack. Ensure there is good air circulation around it. If hanging, use a padded hanger to avoid creases or marks. Turn the item periodically to promote even drying. Air drying can take longer than machine drying, especially for thicker pillows. Be patient and allow the item to dry completely before use. Any residual moisture can lead to mildew, which is hard to remove. This careful drying process helps maintain the pillow's shape and integrity. ### Effective Spot Cleaning for Non-Washable Pillows

Many throw pillows are labeled "dry clean only" or have inserts that cannot be submerged in water. For these items, or for small, isolated spills, spot cleaning is your best friend. This method targets specific areas without wetting the entire pillow, preventing potential damage like water rings or shrinking. I use spot cleaning frequently to keep my delicate pillows looking pristine between professional cleanings.

To begin spot cleaning, gather your supplies: a mild detergent, a bowl of cool water, and several clean, white cloths or sponges. You can use a gentle laundry detergent, dish soap, or even a specialized upholstery cleaner. Always perform a patch test on an inconspicuous area first. This confirms the cleaning solution will not damage or discolor the fabric. Once tested, proceed to the stained area. Dampen a clean cloth with the cleaning solution. Blot the stain gently from the outside in. Avoid rubbing, as this can spread the stain or damage the fabric fibers. Apply light pressure and lift the stain bit by bit.

After applying the cleaning solution, use a separate clean cloth dampened with plain water. Blot the area to rinse away any soap residue. This step is crucial to prevent water marks or detergent buildup. Finish by blotting with a dry cloth to absorb as much moisture as possible. Allow the area to air dry completely. #### Specific Stain Solutions

Different stains require different approaches. For general dirt or spills, the mild soap and water method usually works. For grease stains, a small amount of cornstarch or baking soda can absorb the oil before you blot it away. Sprinkle it on, let it sit for 15-30 minutes, then brush it off. Then proceed with the mild soap and water method.

For ink stains, rubbing alcohol or hairspray on a clean cloth can sometimes dissolve the ink. Always blot, never rub. For pet messes, enzyme cleaners are highly effective at breaking down organic matter and eliminating odors. No matter the stain type, act quickly. Fresh stains are much easier to remove than old, set-in ones. Always work in a well-ventilated area. After cleaning, ensure the pillow is completely dry to prevent mildew.

### Refreshing and Maintaining Your Throw Pillows

Beyond deep cleaning, regular maintenance keeps your throw pillows looking their best. Consistent care prolongs their life and keeps your living space fresh. I have found that a little effort often saves a lot of time in the long run. My routine involves several simple steps that make a big difference.

First, regular dusting and vacuuming are essential. Dust accumulates on fabric surfaces, dulling colors and harboring allergens. Use a soft-bristled brush attachment on your vacuum cleaner to gently vacuum the pillow surfaces. This removes surface dust, crumbs, and pet hair. Doing this once a week prevents deep-seated grime. Second, fluff your pillows daily. Over time, pillows flatten and lose their shape. A good fluffing helps redistribute the filling, maintaining fullness and preventing permanent indentations. Simply punch and plump them from different sides.

Third, consider using fabric fresheners for a quick refresh. A light mist of a fabric-safe spray can neutralize odors and add a pleasant scent. Choose sprays designed for upholstery to avoid damage. For stronger odors, place pillows outdoors on a sunny, breezy day for a few hours. Sunlight and fresh air naturally disinfect and deodorize fabrics. Remember to bring them in before evening dew sets. Lastly, establish a cleaning schedule. Not every pillow needs washing every month. However, high-traffic pillows might need more frequent attention than decorative ones. Understanding [how often should you wash your pillows](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-often-should-you-wash-your-pillows) helps create an effective maintenance plan. This holistic approach ensures your throw pillows remain inviting and hygienic.

### Handling Specific Throw Pillow Materials

Different materials require specific handling to ensure they remain beautiful and intact. What works for cotton may harm silk, so knowing your fabric is key. I have learned through experience that treating each material with its unique needs in mind prevents costly mistakes. Let us explore some common and specific types.

**Velvet and Silk Pillows:** These luxurious fabrics demand gentle care. Velvet can develop water spots, so avoid soaking it. For spills, blot immediately with a clean, dry cloth. For general cleaning, light vacuuming with an upholstery attachment is best. For deeper cleaning, steam cleaning can refresh velvet without water marks. Use a garment steamer at a distance. Silk should only be hand washed or dry cleaned. If hand washing, use cold water and a delicate silk detergent. Avoid wringing or twisting. Lay flat to dry away from direct sunlight.

**Wool Pillows:** Wool is known for its durability but can shrink if washed improperly. Always use cold water and a wool-specific detergent. Hand wash gently, avoiding harsh agitation. After washing, press out excess water and reshape. Lay wool pillows flat to dry on a clean towel. Never put wool in a dryer; the heat will cause irreversible shrinking.

**Embellished Pillows:** Pillows with beads, sequins, embroidery, or delicate trims usually require spot cleaning only. Machine washing or even hand washing can dislodge embellishments or snag threads. Use a damp cloth with mild soap, carefully cleaning around the decorations. If an embellishment comes loose, reattach it promptly with fabric glue or a needle and thread.

**Outdoor Throw Pillows:** These pillows are designed to withstand outdoor elements. Many outdoor pillow covers can be hosed down with water and scrubbed with a mild detergent. They are often made from fade-resistant and mold-resistant materials. Always check the label, but typically, these are the easiest to clean. Ensure they dry thoroughly in the sun to prevent mildew growth. Remember, proper care tailored to the material ensures your pillows look great for years.

## FAQ Section

### 1. Can you put throw pillows in the washing machine?
You can machine wash throw pillows or their covers if the care tag explicitly says it is machine washable. Always use cold water and a gentle cycle to protect the fabric and color. Place covers in a mesh laundry bag for added protection. Inserts are usually only machine washable if made of synthetic fiberfill.

### 2. How do you clean throw pillows without removing the cover?
For pillows with non-removable covers or delicate fabrics, spot cleaning is the best method. Use a mild detergent solution and a clean cloth to blot the stained area. Rinse with a damp cloth and blot dry. For overall refreshing, vacuum the pillow and use a fabric freshener.

### 3. How often should I clean my throw pillows?
The cleaning frequency depends on use. High-traffic pillows or those used by pets might need cleaning every 1-3 months. Decorative pillows in less-used areas might only need cleaning every 6-12 months. Regular dusting and spot cleaning can extend the time between full washes.

### 4. What is the best way to dry throw pillows?
Air drying is the safest method for throw pillows and covers. Lay them flat on a clean surface or hang them on a drying rack. If using a machine dryer for covers, select the lowest heat setting. Ensure all parts are completely dry before use to prevent mildew.

### 5. Can I use a steam cleaner on my throw pillows?
You can use a steam cleaner on some throw pillows, especially those made of velvet or other upholstery fabrics. Steam can refresh fabric and kill dust mites. Always test on an inconspicuous area first. Keep the steamer moving and do not saturate the fabric. Avoid steam cleaning delicate materials like silk.

### 6. How do I remove odors from throw pillows?
To remove odors, first ensure the pillow is clean. For light odors, sprinkle baking soda over the pillow, let it sit for several hours, then vacuum it off. For stronger odors, placing pillows outdoors on a sunny, breezy day can help. Fabric freshening sprays also work for a quick refresh.
