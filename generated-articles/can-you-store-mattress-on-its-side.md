## Storing Your Mattress on Its Side: A Comprehensive Guide

Moving house or clearing out a spare room often leaves us asking big questions. One common question is: "Can you store a mattress on its side?" It feels like a convenient way to save space. However, this storage method is not always straightforward. Improper vertical storage can lead to significant damage to your mattress.

I often wonder about the best way to keep my mattress safe when I am not using it. This guide explores the practice of storing mattresses on their side. We will cover different mattress types, potential risks, and the best techniques to ensure your mattress stays in good shape. You will learn how to prepare your mattress, secure it, and avoid common storage mistakes. Let's make sure your mattress remains comfortable for years to come.

### Takeaway:

*   Most mattresses can be stored on their side for short periods if done correctly.
*   Memory foam and latex mattresses are more prone to damage from vertical storage.
*   Always support the mattress edges and prevent bending or sagging.
*   Clean, wrap, and elevate your mattress to protect it from moisture and pests.
*   Long-term vertical storage should be avoided if possible for most mattress types.

### Can You Store Mattress On Its Side?

Yes, you can store a mattress on its side, but with important caveats. This method works best for short-term storage and certain mattress types. Proper support and preparation are crucial to prevent damage like sagging, warping, or material displacement. Always consider the mattress type and planned storage duration.

## Understanding Mattress Types and Vertical Storage

Different mattresses react differently to being stored on their side. Understanding your mattress type is the first step. This knowledge helps you decide if vertical storage is a good option. It also helps you learn how to protect your investment.

### Innerspring Mattresses and Side Storage

Innerspring mattresses have a core of metal coils. These coils provide support. Storing an innerspring mattress on its side can put stress on these coils. The coils are designed to bear weight evenly across their surface. When you store the mattress vertically, the weight rests on a small edge. This can cause some coils to compress or bend over time. This leads to permanent unevenness in the mattress.

For short periods, such as during a move, innerspring mattresses can usually handle being on their side. You must ensure the mattress has full support along its bottom edge. Avoid leaning it against an uneven surface. Use a sturdy wall or multiple heavy items for support.

### Memory Foam Mattresses and Vertical Storage

Memory foam mattresses are known for their body-conforming properties. They consist of dense foam layers. These layers are designed to distribute body weight evenly. Storing a memory foam mattress on its side poses more risks. The foam can slowly deform under its own weight when vertical. This means the foam can permanently sag or warp. This damage happens because memory foam is very pliable. It does not have a rigid internal structure like coils.

Manufacturers often advise against vertical storage for memory foam. If you must store it vertically, keep the period very short. Make sure it stands perfectly straight. This helps distribute the weight evenly down the side. Using a mattress bag is also a good idea. This can help keep its shape somewhat contained.

### Latex Mattresses and Vertical Storage

Latex mattresses are durable and resilient. They are made from natural or synthetic latex foam. While more resilient than memory foam, latex mattresses also face challenges with side storage. Latex can be quite heavy and flexible. Over time, its weight can cause it to compress and deform when stored on an edge. This leads to a permanent indent or unevenness in the mattress structure. The internal cell structure of latex is not designed for vertical pressure.

Similar to memory foam, vertical storage for latex mattresses should be minimal. Ensure the mattress is completely flat against a solid surface. This helps spread the load. Using a robust mattress bag can also offer some support. The goal is to prevent any bending or creasing of the material.

### Hybrid Mattresses and Vertical Storage

Hybrid mattresses combine different technologies. They often have an innerspring core with memory foam or latex layers on top. This blend presents a unique storage challenge. The coil base might offer more structural integrity than pure foam. However, the foam layers are still vulnerable to deformation. The weight of the foam layers can pull on the coils. This creates stress points. It can also cause the foam to separate from the coil base.

For hybrids, vertical storage carries moderate risk. The duration of storage is a key factor. Short-term vertical storage is generally acceptable. Always provide firm, even support along the bottom edge. Wrapping the mattress tightly can also help keep all layers together.

## Risks of Storing a Mattress on Its Side

Storing your mattress vertically might seem like a smart space-saving solution. However, this method carries several risks. Understanding these risks helps you make an informed decision. It also helps you take proper precautions to protect your mattress.

### Structural Damage and Sagging

The main risk of vertical mattress storage is structural damage. Mattresses are built to support weight horizontally. When you place a mattress on its side, its entire weight rests on a narrow edge. This puts immense pressure on the internal components. For innerspring mattresses, coils can bend or become permanently compressed. This leads to lumps and unevenness. Memory foam and latex mattresses can deform. They might develop permanent sags or divots along the bottom edge. This damage affects the mattress's ability to provide proper support. A damaged mattress will not offer a comfortable night's sleep.

This kind of damage often reduces the lifespan of the mattress. It also impacts sleep quality. The internal materials shift, creating uncomfortable spots. You might notice the mattress feels different after being stored on its side for a long time.

### Warping and Loss of Shape

Warping is another significant concern. This happens when the mattress does not stay perfectly straight. If the mattress leans or bends even slightly, it can lose its original shape. Memory foam and latex are especially prone to this. Gravity pulls on the material, causing it to distort. The mattress might develop a permanent curve or bow. This makes the mattress unfit for use. It will not lie flat on a bed frame. A warped mattress cannot provide uniform support to your body.

Even a slight warp can affect how the mattress fits on your bed. It can also make sheets and mattress protectors difficult to use. This kind of damage is often irreversible. It means you might need to replace the mattress sooner than planned.

### Material Compression and Density Changes

When a mattress is stored vertically, the internal materials are under constant compression. This is especially true for the foam layers in memory foam, latex, and hybrid mattresses. The foam can become denser in the compressed areas. This alters the feel and performance of the mattress. Parts of the mattress might feel harder or softer than others. This change in density affects comfort. It also impacts the mattress's ability to relieve pressure points. The foam layers might also separate from each other.

This compression can be uneven. It creates pressure differences across the mattress surface. This can lead to discomfort and poor sleep. The mattress loses its original factory-designed consistency. Restoring it to its original state is often impossible.

### Airflow and Moisture Concerns

Storing a mattress on its side, especially if it is tightly wrapped, can restrict airflow. This creates a risk for moisture buildup. Mattresses need to breathe to prevent mold and mildew. If the mattress is stored in a humid environment and cannot breathe, moisture can get trapped. This moisture becomes a breeding ground for mold. Mold not only smells bad but also poses health risks. It can also degrade the mattress materials.

To counter this, ensure the storage area is dry and well-ventilated. Using a breathable mattress bag is key. Avoid wrapping the mattress in regular plastic sheeting for long periods. This can trap moisture inside. Elevating the mattress slightly off the floor also helps with air circulation.

### Warranty Voiding

Many mattress manufacturers have specific guidelines for storage. Storing a mattress on its side, especially for extended periods, can void its warranty. Manufacturers design mattresses for horizontal use. Any damage resulting from vertical storage might not be covered. It is always wise to check your mattress warranty terms before storing it vertically. Reading the fine print can save you from future issues.

Protecting your warranty is protecting your investment. If you suspect you might need to make a warranty claim, proper storage is vital. Always follow the manufacturer's advice. This ensures your mattress remains covered.

## Best Practices for Temporary Vertical Mattress Storage

Sometimes, storing a mattress on its side is unavoidable. Perhaps you are moving, or you need to clear space for a short period. In these situations, following best practices can minimize risks. Proper preparation is key to safeguarding your mattress's integrity.

### Short-Term Storage Considerations

If you plan to store your mattress vertically for a short time (a few days to a couple of weeks), the risks are lower. My primary goal is always to prevent any bending or uneven pressure. Make sure the mattress stands perfectly upright. It needs full support along its bottom edge. Leaning it against a wall at an angle can cause permanent curves. Use a sturdy, flat surface for the base. A strong wall works well for the back support.

Consider the environment too. A cool, dry, and clean area is best. Avoid garages or basements if they are prone to humidity. This prevents mold growth during temporary storage.

### Preparing Your Mattress for Side Storage

Proper preparation is vital before you store any mattress vertically. First, clean your mattress thoroughly. Vacuum both sides to remove dust, dirt, and debris. Spot clean any stains with a mild cleaner and let it air dry completely. Moisture is the enemy of stored mattresses. Next, enclose the mattress in a high-quality mattress bag. These bags protect against dust, dirt, moisture, and pests. Choose a durable bag made from thick plastic or breathable fabric.

You can often find suitable bags at moving supply stores. This step is critical for cleanliness and hygiene. It also offers a minor level of structural containment. If you are storing a [guest mattress](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-store-guest-mattress), ensuring it is pristine before bagging will make it ready for use when needed.

### Choosing the Right Storage Location

The environment where you store your mattress truly matters. Select a location that is climate-controlled. This means it has stable temperature and humidity levels. Avoid areas with extreme temperature fluctuations. High humidity encourages mold and mildew. Low humidity can dry out materials. Basements, attics, and non-climate-controlled storage units are often unsuitable. These places frequently have moisture problems. They can also have pest issues.

A spare room in your home or a climate-controlled storage facility is ideal. Ensure the space is clean and free of pests. Elevate the mattress slightly off the floor using wood planks or pallets. This protects against ground moisture and improves air circulation.

### Securing the Mattress Against Movement

A mattress stored vertically must be stable. It cannot fall or shift. This is for safety and to prevent damage. Secure the mattress to a sturdy wall or another solid object. Use furniture straps or strong ropes. Make sure the straps are tight but do not compress the mattress too much. The goal is to prevent tipping. A falling mattress can cause serious injury. It can also damage the mattress itself.

Placing heavy objects on either side of the mattress base can add stability. Just make sure they do not damage the mattress cover. The mattress should feel solid and unmoving when you try to gently push it.

### Regular Inspection During Storage

Even with the best preparation, regular checks are important for longer short-term storage. Inspect your mattress periodically. Look for any signs of sagging, warping, or moisture. Check for mold, mildew, or unpleasant odors. Catching problems early can prevent further damage. If you notice any issues, address them immediately. You might need to adjust the mattress's position. You might also need to improve ventilation.

This is especially true if you are storing your mattress for several weeks. A quick check every month or so will give you peace of mind. It also protects your investment.

## Proper Support and Positioning for Vertical Storage

Achieving proper support and positioning is crucial when storing a mattress on its side. Without it, you risk permanent damage. This section provides detailed steps to ensure your mattress remains intact and comfortable for future use. My aim is to make sure your mattress comes out of storage as good as it went in.

### Providing a Flat, Sturdy Base

The very first step is to provide an absolutely flat and sturdy base for the mattress. The entire bottom edge of the mattress must rest evenly on the floor. Do not allow any part of the edge to hang over. This creates uneven pressure points. Use a clean, level section of the floor. If your floor is uneven, place a solid piece of plywood or several sturdy wooden planks underneath. These should cover the full length of the mattress edge. This distributes the mattress's weight uniformly.

An uneven base is one of the fastest ways to cause localized compression and damage. Ensure this base is stable and will not shift. This protects the vulnerable edge of the mattress.

### Supporting the Mattress Vertically

Once the base is secure, you need to support the mattress vertically. It should stand perfectly upright. Do not lean it at an angle against a wall. Leaning causes the mattress to bend or bow over time. This leads to permanent deformation. Position the mattress flat against a solid, flat wall. The wall should be free of any protrusions or unevenness. These could dent the mattress.

For extra stability, consider using furniture straps to secure the mattress to the wall studs. This prevents it from tipping over. A mattress standing perfectly straight helps its internal components maintain their original alignment. This is vital for all mattress types, especially those with coils.

### Preventing Bending and Creasing

Bending and creasing are major threats to a vertically stored mattress. This is particularly true for memory foam and latex. To prevent this, ensure the mattress is held flat. You can place sturdy, flat boards along the sides if you fear it might flex. These boards act as external splints. They prevent the mattress from bowing in the middle. The boards should be as tall as the mattress itself. They should also be long enough to cover most of its length.

Tight mattress bags also provide some containment. They offer a minimal external force to keep the mattress straight. But they are not a substitute for rigid support. Think of it as bracing. The more uniformly you can keep the mattress straight, the better. This is especially true if you are storing a [guest mattress](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-store-guest-mattress) for an extended period, as it might see infrequent checks.

### Using External Support Frames (If Available)

For long-term vertical storage, or for very heavy mattresses, an external support frame is ideal. Some storage companies offer specialized frames. These frames hold the mattress perfectly upright and flat. They ensure the weight is evenly distributed. These are designed to mimic horizontal support but in a vertical orientation. If you have access to such a frame, use it. It is the best way to prevent damage during vertical storage.

These frames usually involve a base and side supports. They keep the mattress from flexing. This is a good option for expensive mattresses or if you need to store it vertically for many months.

### Avoiding Stacking Other Items Against It

Never stack other items directly against the vertically stored mattress. This adds uneven pressure. Even light boxes can cause indentations over time. The mattress needs its own clear space. It should not be part of a stack. Any item pressing against it can cause a permanent change in shape or density. This undermines all other efforts to store it safely.

Keep a small clearance around the mattress if possible. This also helps with air circulation. The goal is to allow the mattress to stand freely, supported only by its base and the flat wall.

## Alternative Storage Solutions When Vertical Isn't Ideal

Sometimes, storing a mattress on its side is simply not the best option. Or perhaps, the risks outweigh the benefits for your specific mattress type or storage duration. Knowing alternative storage methods can save your mattress from damage. My preference is always the safest method possible for my belongings.

### Flat Storage on a Pallet or Platform

The safest way to store any mattress is flat. This mimics how it is used on a bed frame. Laying the mattress flat ensures its weight is distributed evenly across its entire surface. This prevents any single area from bearing excessive load. Use a clean, dry pallet or a raised platform. This keeps the mattress off the ground. Getting it off the floor protects against ground moisture, cold, and pests. The pallet also allows for air circulation underneath. This helps prevent mold and mildew.

Ensure the pallet or platform is slightly larger than the mattress. This provides full support to all edges. This method is especially recommended for memory foam, latex, and hybrid mattresses. It helps them retain their shape and integrity over time.

### Using Climate-Controlled Storage Units

If you lack suitable space at home, a climate-controlled storage unit is an excellent option. These units maintain stable temperature and humidity levels. This protects your mattress from extreme heat, cold, and moisture. Such conditions are crucial for preserving mattress materials. High humidity causes mold, while extreme temperatures can degrade foams and adhesives. A climate-controlled environment mitigates these risks effectively.

When storing in a unit, still use a mattress bag and a pallet. Even in a good unit, extra protection is always a wise choice. This combines the benefits of flat storage with optimal environmental conditions. This is my top recommendation for long-term mattress storage.

### Rolling a Mattress (For Specific Types Only)

Some specific types of mattresses can be rolled for storage. This usually applies to newer memory foam or foam mattresses that came compressed in a box. These mattresses are designed to be rolled and unrolled. They can often be rolled back up using compression bags. Always check the manufacturer's instructions. Not all foam mattresses are meant to be re-rolled after expansion. Attempting to roll an innerspring or hybrid mattress will cause severe, irreversible damage. The coils will bend and break.

If your mattress can be rolled, it offers a compact storage solution. Ensure it is tightly wrapped and secured. This prevents it from unrolling or expanding prematurely. This method is great for saving space if your mattress type allows it.

### Protecting the Mattress During Transit

When moving your mattress, especially if it must be on its side, proper protection is key. Use a sturdy mattress bag designed for moving. These bags are often thicker and more durable than regular storage bags. They protect against dirt, tears, and minor abrasions. Secure the mattress firmly in the moving truck or vehicle. Use ropes or straps to prevent it from shifting. Movement during transit can cause internal damage.

If the mattress must stand on its side in the truck, ensure it is fully supported. Place it against a flat, rigid surface. Pad any areas where it might rub against other items. This prevents external damage. Careful handling during a move can prevent many storage-related issues.

## When to Avoid Storing Your Mattress Vertically

While temporary vertical storage is sometimes possible, there are situations where it is best avoided entirely. Ignoring these warnings can lead to irreversible damage. My advice is always to prioritize the longevity of your mattress.

### Long-Term Storage (> 1 Month)

Long-term vertical storage, generally anything over one month, should be avoided. The longer a mattress stands on its side, the greater the risk of damage. Gravity continuously pulls on the materials. This leads to sagging, warping, and material compression. These issues become more pronounced with time. Manufacturers design mattresses for horizontal weight distribution. Prolonged vertical stress goes against this design.

If you need to store your mattress for several months or longer, always opt for flat storage. A climate-controlled storage unit with the mattress laid flat on a pallet is the safest option. This protects your mattress for extended periods.

### Mattresses with Known Structural Weaknesses

Some mattresses might already have structural weaknesses. These could be older mattresses or those with minor damage. Storing such a mattress vertically will only worsen the problem. Any existing sags or soft spots will become more pronounced. The stress of vertical storage will likely cause further breakdown of the internal components. It will accelerate its deterioration.

If your mattress is showing signs of age or wear, avoid putting it on its side. It is better to dispose of it or store it flat. You risk destroying it completely if you store it vertically.

### Extremely Heavy or Large Mattresses

Very heavy or large mattresses (like king or California king sizes) are generally not suitable for vertical storage. Their immense weight puts even greater strain on the bottom edge. This increases the risk of severe compression and warping. Handling such heavy mattresses vertically is also more difficult. It poses a safety risk to the person moving them. A large, heavy mattress is hard to keep perfectly straight.

For these larger and heavier mattresses, flat storage is almost always the only safe option. If you are moving a [guest mattress](https://homeessentialsguide.com/how-to-store-guest-mattress) of a larger size, plan carefully for horizontal transport and storage.

### Mattresses with Pillow Tops

Mattresses with pillow tops or euro tops have extra layers of padding sewn into the top. These layers are soft and plush. When stored vertically, these extra layers can shift and compress. They can also create permanent indentations. The delicate padding is not designed to bear the entire mattress weight. This can ruin the comfort and feel of the pillow top. The mattress will lose its luxurious softness.

If your mattress has a pillow top, always store it flat. This preserves the integrity of the comfort layers. Vertical storage for these types is highly discouraged.

### When Warranty Conditions Prohibit Vertical Storage

Before storing your mattress, review its warranty. Many mattress warranties specifically exclude damage from improper storage. This often includes vertical storage. If your warranty states that vertical storage voids the coverage, do not do it. Following warranty terms protects your investment. It ensures you can make a claim if a defect arises.

Ignoring warranty conditions can be costly. You might find yourself with a damaged mattress and no recourse for replacement or repair. Always check the fine print to protect your purchase.

## FAQ Section

### How long can a memory foam mattress be stored on its side?
Memory foam mattresses should only be stored on their side for very short periods. A few days during a move is generally acceptable. Any longer, like a few weeks or months, greatly increases the risk of permanent sagging or warping. Flat storage is always best for memory foam to maintain its integrity.

### Does storing a mattress vertically void its warranty?
Yes, storing a mattress vertically can void its warranty. Most manufacturers design mattresses for horizontal use and specify storage conditions. Damage from improper vertical storage is often not covered. Always check your specific mattress warranty terms before choosing a vertical storage method to avoid issues.

### How do I prevent mold when storing a mattress?
To prevent mold, ensure the mattress is completely dry before storage. Wrap it in a breathable mattress bag, not regular plastic sheeting. Store it in a climate-controlled, dry environment. Elevate the mattress off the floor using pallets for air circulation. Regular inspections for moisture are also helpful.

### What's the best way to move a mattress on its side?
When moving a mattress on its side, first enclose it in a durable mattress moving bag. Keep it standing perfectly upright, without bending. Use furniture straps to secure it to the side of a moving truck or vehicle to prevent shifting. Always have at least two people to carry it carefully.

### Can box springs be stored on their side?
Yes, box springs can generally be stored on their side with fewer risks than mattresses. Box springs have a more rigid frame. However, ensure they are supported evenly along the bottom edge. Avoid leaning them at an angle for long periods. This can cause the internal wood frame to warp over time.

### How do I protect mattress edges during vertical storage?
To protect mattress edges, ensure the entire bottom edge rests on a flat, sturdy surface. Place it against a strong, flat wall for vertical support. Use furniture straps to secure it, preventing any bending or flexing. Avoid stacking items near or on the mattress, as this can compress the edges.

## Conclusion

Storing a mattress on its side requires careful thought and proper execution. While it offers a convenient space-saving solution, it is not without risks. My experience tells me that understanding your mattress type is key. Innerspring mattresses handle vertical storage better than memory foam or latex, but all require good support. The biggest dangers include structural damage, warping, and material compression. These can permanently alter your mattress's comfort and support.

I recommend vertical storage only for short durations. Always prepare your mattress by cleaning and bagging it. Choose a dry, stable location. Provide full, even support along the mattress's entire bottom edge and length. If possible, opt for flat storage on a pallet in a climate-controlled environment. This is the safest bet for long-term preservation. Protecting your mattress ensures it remains a comfortable place to rest for many years. Make an informed decision to extend the life of your mattress.
